The ProblemWhy legal AI is hard

Legal answers cannot be judged by fluency. They must be grounded in identifiable sources, carry valid article-level citations, stay inside the right jurisdiction, respect legal structure and time — and abstain when the law is silent.

Where fluent LLMs fail in law

Hallucinated citations Attribution gap Foreign-law contamination No abstention Retrieval blindness Multi-hop fragility

Algerian law is a demanding case

Predominantly Arabic Historically layered 1963–2025 Structurally complex Low-resource in legal AI

Research gap

No Algerian-law system jointly handles article-level citation faithfulness, amendment-aware structure, jurisdictional discipline and principled abstention. Scaling the model alone does not fix it.

Objective

Build AKN-RLM — an architecture that grounds every answer in verifiable Algerian legal sources, and declines when the corpus does not justify an answer.

Citation faithfulness findings

The only system to hold both hallucinated-citation and jurisdictional-infection rates at 0.000. Gemini 2.5 Flash still imports foreign law on 15 of 40 traps (JIR 0.375); Qwen 2 falls into 27 (JIR 0.675).

Reliability tracks legal structure, not parametric scale — a smaller controller leads, while the largest baselines are the most hallucination-prone.

Limitations

  • Retrieval ceiling: Recall@10 = 0.258 (gold in top-10 on ~¼ of queries)
  • French under-served — Arabic-primary system
  • Abstention precision 0.654
  • Metrics are proxies, not expert review

Future work

  • Corpus-tuned dense embedder
  • French ↔ Arabic retrieval
  • Graph-retrieval redesign
  • Faithfulness-gate ablations
  • Expert legal validation
The takeaway

Trustworthy legal AI must be built as an evidence-controlled system, not an unconstrained generator — it requires structured legal sources, controlled retrieval, recursive reasoning, and strict citation verification.
