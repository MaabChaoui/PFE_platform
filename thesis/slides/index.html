Landscape & Limits
why legal AI fails — and why Algeria is the hard case
The problem

In law, fluency is not the standard. Justification is.

a real legal source the correct article a valid citation …or a principled abstention
The legal domain through an AI lens

Legal meaning is not linguistic meaning

  • iStructured — codes, chapters, numbered articles; structure carries meaning
  • iiInterdependent — definitions, exceptions and cross-references live in other articles
  • iiiTime-sensitive — amendments decide which version is law today
  • ivAuthority-bound — an answer must cite the provision that makes it true
HenceStructure is an asset when machine-readable — and a liability when buried inside publication-oriented PDFs.
The low-resource condition

Algerian law as a stress test

  • ·Legal-AI research concentrates on English, well-resourced systems — LexGLUE, LegalBench, COLIEE.
  • ·Arabic legal AI is emerging (ArabLegalEval, AraLegal-BERT) — but citation-controlled reasoning for Algerian law is unexplored.
  • ·Before any model: Algeria needs infrastructure — structured corpus, article identifiers, legal links, amendment metadata, a benchmark.
The failure of current AI in law

Hallucination becomes a legal-validity problem

Failure I — Hallucination

Inventing the law

Models fabricate claims, citations and whole authorities while sounding confident — Mata v. Avianca shows fabricated citations entering real legal submissions.

Ji et al. 2023 · Mata 2023
Failure II — The attribution gap

Answering without grounding

Even a true answer is legally weightless if the system cannot show which source supports which claim. Attribution is a requirement, not a feature.

Rashkin et al. 2023
Failure III — The half-measure

Citing without justifying

Citation-aware generation is improving — but an emitted citation does not guarantee the article exists, is in force, or actually supports the claim.

Zhang et al. 2025
Fluency, truth, attribution, justification — each is a strictly stronger requirement. Law demands the last one.

Pillar I of V · the corpus

Akoma Ntoso — giving law a machine-readable body

  • ·OASIS XML standard for legal documents: hierarchy, metadata, cross-references, temporal versions.
  • ·Each article becomes a stable, citable unit (eId) — the anchor for retrieval, the KG, and citation checking.
  • ·PDFs are readable by humans — they hide article boundaries, hierarchy, references, amendment history.
  • ·In Algerian law, structuring is a precondition, not preprocessing — nothing downstream exists without it.

Pillar III of V · relations

Legal Knowledge Graphs — because law is relational

  • ·Provisions cite, amend, define, limit and depend on other provisions — a graph, not a list of passages.
  • ·GraphRAG: relations guide evidence expansion where text similarity is blind — exceptions and amendments never “resemble” the query. Edge et al. 2025
  • ·Temporality is the open challenge: the graph must answer “what was the law on this date?” Kucuk & Can 2025
  • ·Ours is derived from the structured corpus — not manually invented.
Pillar IIIThe graph supplies what similarity cannot — the relational and temporal context of every article.
Pillar IV of V · inspectability

Argument structure — evidence you can inspect

Each cited provision becomes an ADU triplet — claims, grounds, warrants, rebuttals, backing — so support can be checked unit by unit before the final answer is written.

Pillar IVToulmin, Walton, ASPIC⁺ — legal reasoning should expose its structure, including where it can be defeated.
Pillar V of V · control

Recursive Language Models — the orchestration layer

  • ·The RLM can decompose the question, inspect evidence, call retrieval, traverse the graph, verify support, and revise its path. Zhang et al. 2025
  • ·A control abstraction over retrieval, RAG and the KG — not a replacement for them.
  • ·Not an autonomous legal judge: every step is bounded, logged, and grounded in deterministic infrastructure.
Pillar VRecursion buys the right to look again — under a budget, over infrastructure that cannot hallucinate.

Retrieval at its limits

Retrieval finds passages. Law requires sufficiency.

Sparse — BM25

Wins: exact article numbers, law numbers, dates. Fails: lay paraphrase, AR/FR vocabulary mismatch.

Dense — embeddings

Wins: semantic match across phrasings. Fails: exact identifiers, rare legal terms.

Hybrid

They fail differently — fusing them lifts recall. But all three are structure-blind: an exception or amendment never “resembles” the query.

What answering actually takes
Hybrid retrieval raises recall — legal sufficiency still requires structural and temporal checking.

Ablations & honest limits

Where it still breaks

The binding bottleneck

Article-level retrieval

The gold article reaches the top-ten candidates on only ≈ ¼ of queries. The ceiling is infrastructural, not generative.

Cross-lingual fragility

French is the hard slice

The French-translated benchmark exposes degradation — today this is an Arabic-primary system, not yet a bilingual assistant.

Gate trade-offs

Strictness has a price

The faithfulness gate helps rule-application and multi-hop questions — but over-rejects layman-language queries. Removing the jurisdiction gate drops abstention recall on traps.

The MultiQuery paradox

More candidates ≠ better citations

MultiQuery lifts retrieval metrics yet lowers final Citation F1 — wider evidence pools dilute citation discipline.

Strongest: exact-article & unanswerable. Hardest: multi-hop, long-context, French. We say so in the thesis — and the jury should hear it from us first.

Knowing when not to answer

four behaviours observed in the qualitative study
  • iClean success — correct article, supported claims, valid citations
  • iiPartial multi-hop failure — first hop right, chain broken downstream
  • iiiTemporal-recursive reasoning — the controller walks the amendment history to the applicable version
  • ivPrincipled abstention — the gate refuses, and states why
In law, refusal is a safety behaviour, not a failure — a wrong answer with a confident citation is the worst outcome.
