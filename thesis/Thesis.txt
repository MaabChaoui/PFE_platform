Abstract
Artificial intelligence has advanced rapidly in the legal field, yet Large Language Models
remain unreliable for low-resource, non-English legal systems. Legal answers cannot be
judged by fluency or plausibility alone: they must be grounded in identifiable sources,
supported by valid article-level citations, bounded by the correct jurisdiction, sensitive to
legal structure and temporality, and able to abstain when the corpus does not justify an
answer. Algerian law is a particularly demanding case — predominantly Arabic, historically layered, structurally complex, and markedly underrepresented in existing legal-AI
resources.
This thesis introduces AKN-RLM, an architecture for citation-faithful legal reasoning
in Algerian law that integrates Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Akoma Ntoso legaldocument structuring, a Legal Knowledge Graph, Toulmin-based Argument Mining,
and bounded Recursive Language Model orchestration behind a triple faithfulness
gate enforcing citation existence, jurisdictional discipline, and claim-level support.
Two novel resources underpin the work. The first is a structured Algerian legal corpus of
171 files spanning 1963–2025, pairing source documents with Akoma Ntoso XML and
RDF/Turtle representations across constitutional texts, major codes, sectoral legislation,
and amending decrees. The second is AlgerianLegalBench, a benchmark of 244 questions
across 23 legal categories and 8 query types, evaluating retrieval, citation accuracy,
abstention, and faithfulness, including forty jurisdictional-infection traps that test whether
a system imports foreign-law doctrine where Algerian law is silent.
Under realistic classifier-routed deployment, AKN-RLM reaches Citation F1 = 0.305
while holding both the hallucinated-citation rate and the jurisdictional-infection rate at
0.000 and reaching abstention F1 = 0.703. It improves Citation F1 by 1.64×, 2.9×,
and 1.74× over the strongest direct-LLM, deterministic-retrieval, and minimal-RAG baselines respectively, and is the only compared system capable of principled abstention. Reliability tracks legal structure, not parametric scale: the 70B baseline is among the most
hallucination-prone, while AKN-RLM leads with a smaller controller. These gains hold
against a binding retrieval ceiling — the gold article reaches the top ten on only about
a quarter of queries — which localizes the central finding: in the Algerian legal context,
the main barrier to reliable legal AI is not only algorithmic but infrastructural.
Keywords: Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Akoma Ntoso, Legal Knowledge Graph,
Argument Mining, Citation Faithfulness, Abstention, Legal AI, Arabic NLP, Algerian
Law, Recursive Language Models, Legal Question Answering.

General Introduction
In light of the rapid transformations the world is witnessing in the fields of digitalization and Artificial Intelligence (AI), legal systems are required to keep pace with these
developments to ensure greater effectiveness in the production and dissemination of legal
knowledge. Legal reasoning is a fundamental pillar of judicial and scholarly work, as it
relies on analysing legal texts, interpreting them, and linking them to case law and general
principles in order to arrive at precise and consistent rulings. However, this reasoning, in
its traditional form, faces increasing challenges related to the proliferation of legal texts,
the multiplicity of their sources, and the diﬀiculty of accessing accurate legal citations in
a timely manner. The intersection of AI and law has garnered increasing attention from
academic and industrial circles over the past decade. Tasks that were once the exclusive
domain of trained legal professionals—such as retrieving legal texts, answering legal questions, analysing contracts, and conducting case-based reasoning—are increasingly being
performed through Natural Language Processing (NLP) and machine learning.
The risks this entails are not hypothetical. The case of Mata v. Avianca, Inc., 2023
has become one of the clearest illustrations of what is at stake when AI systems enter
legal contexts without adequate safeguards. In that case, legal arguments were supported
by fictitious judicial decisions generated by ChatGPT and submitted without proper verification. More than a widely cited anecdote, this episode exposed a structural mismatch
between what contemporary AI does well and what legal reasoning requires. LLMs can
produce fluent, plausible, and confident answers, but the law demands something stricter:
accuracy, traceability, stability, and justification grounded in authoritative sources.
Performance measurement initiatives such as LexGLUE (Chalkidis et al., 2022),
LegalBench (Guha et al., 2023), and Competition on Legal Information Extraction/Entailment (COLIEE) (Goebel et al., 2024) have significantly contributed to the
development of this field. However, most of this progress has focused on a limited set of
legal systems, particularly English and other resource-rich languages.
As a result, legal traditions in the Global South, especially in the Arab world, remain
underrepresented in the datasets and inference systems used (Ariai et al., 2024; Niklaus
et al., 2024; Pham and Phan, 2026). While recent efforts such as ArabLegalEval (Hijazi et al., 2024) and ALARB (Abu Shairah et al., 2025), and specialised models like

AraLegal-BERT (AL-Qurishi et al., 2022), indicate a growing interest in Arabic legal
AI, the field is still in its early stages.
In the Algerian context, this challenge is further complicated by the distinctive character of the legal system, whose architecture reflects three overlapping traditions: French
civil law inherited from the colonial period, Islamic jurisprudence, and post-independence
republican codification. Together, these shape a legal corpus that is both linguistically
and structurally demanding. This necessitates the development of modern tools to assist researchers, judges, and legal practitioners in improving the quality and expediting
the legal reasoning process (Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024). The Journal Oﬀiciel de la
République Algérienne (JORA) contains thousands of legal texts, including laws, decrees,
orders, and regulations, most of which are published in Arabic following the Arabisation
reforms of the 1990s. Hence, the importance of employing AI techniques becomes evident,
particularly those related to NLP, the technical precision of legal language, the hierarchical organisation of legislative texts, and the extensive network of references, amendments,
and exceptions that constitute legal meaning.
These challenges are exacerbated by the limitations of contemporary large-scale language models. While models such as Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT)-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) and Arabic-oriented systems like Jais (Sengupta et al., 2023) demonstrate impressive linguistic fluency, they remain unreliable in sensitive legal contexts.
Hallucination—the generation of eloquent but factually inaccurate content—is a welldocumented failure mode in LLMs (Ji et al., 2023). In legal applications, these failures
are not merely technical limitations: a fabricated citation, a deleted clause, or a distorted
article can skew interpretation and undermine confidence in the system. In addition,
recent studies have highlighted the instability of LLMs in legal reasoning tasks, where
repeated prompts can lead to inconsistent answers to the same question (Blair-Stanek
and Van Durme, 2025).
These limitations suggest that progress in legal AI will not be achieved through scaling
alone, but rather through architectures specifically designed for robust, transparent, and
verifiable reasoning. This thesis focuses on developing an integrated framework for legal
reasoning in Algerian law by combining RAG (Lewis et al., 2021; Chia et al., 2024),
Akoma Ntoso-based legal document structuring, KG construction (Zhang et al., 2026),
Argument Mining (AM) (Walton et al., 2008; Habernal et al., 2023; Günzler et al., 2024),
and Recursive Language Models (RLM) orchestration (Zhang et al., 2025a). In this
framework, retrieval grounds the answer in relevant legislative sources, the legal knowledge
graph represents legal entities, concepts, references, and amendment relations, argument
mining organizes legal evidence into more interpretable argumentative structures, and
recursive language-model orchestration coordinates decomposition, retrieval, verification,
and corrective reasoning. This integration aims to improve the accuracy of legal citation,
reduce unsupported generation, and support traceable legal reasoning over Algerian legal

1.1

Introduction

The intersection of law and AI is not merely a field of academic promise; it is also one
in which the consequences of failure are concrete and institutionally significant. Legal
reasoning cannot be reduced to the prediction of likely text or the retrieval of isolated
passages. Legal information is structured, interdependent, and temporally evolving. The
meaning of a provision may depend on a definition located elsewhere in the code, on an
amendment introduced years later, or on its place within a broader normative hierarchy.
These diﬀiculties become even more pronounced in low-resource and under-digitized settings such as Algerian law, where multilingualism, limited computational resources, and
the scarcity of specialised benchmarks make the direct transfer of methods developed for
English-speaking jurisdictions insuﬀicient (Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024).
Against this background, the need for more integrated architectures in legal AI becomes clear. Legal reasoning must be examined through an AI lens that accounts for
the distinctive texture of legal information, the diversity of legal question types, and the
structural constraints that make the Algerian context a particularly demanding case. At
the same time, the principal failure modes of current AI systems when confronted with
that complexity must also be confronted, including hallucination, attribution gaps, retrieval insuﬀiciency, multi-hop reasoning fragility, and the persistent underrepresentation
of non-English legal ecosystems. Taken together, these perspectives show both why legal
AI remains a diﬀicult problem and why its future depends on approaches that are more
grounded, more structured, and more sensitive to the demands of legal reasoning.

The Legal Domain Through an AI Lens

Law is a diﬀicult domain for AI not because legal texts are simply longer, more formal,
or more specialised than ordinary documents, but because legal meaning is structurally
produced. A provision does not operate as an isolated sentence. Its force may depend
on definitions located elsewhere, exceptions introduced in a later paragraph, procedural
conditions specified in another code, or amendments that alter its temporal validity. Legal
reasoning therefore cannot be reduced to the retrieval of semantically similar passages or
the generation of plausible explanatory prose. It requires the reconstruction of a legally
valid path from question to authority, from authority to applicable rule, and from rule to
justified conclusion.
This section identifies the domain properties that make legal reasoning a distinct
computational problem. The aim is not to restate that law is a specialised language,
but to specify which properties of legal material create failure modes for NLP, retrieval,
and generation systems. Four dimensions are central: the internal structure of legal
provisions, the intertextual and temporal organisation of legal corpora, the heterogeneity
of legal questions, and the resource constraints that shape Arabic and Algerian legal AI.

1.2.1

Legal Text as Structured Normative Information

Legal texts are written in natural language, but they do not function as ordinary
natural-language discourse. Their meaning is organised around normative relations: conditions, permissions, obligations, prohibitions, exceptions, sanctions, competences, and
procedures. A legal provision is therefore not merely a unit of information; it is a rulebearing unit whose interpretation depends on its position inside a larger normative structure. Argumentation-theoretic work makes this point explicit: legal discourse is composed
of claims, grounds, warrants, rebuttals, and inferential relations, not only of topically related sentences (Walton et al., 2008; Modgil and Prakken, 2014; Habernal et al., 2023).
This has direct computational consequences. A standard encoder may recognise that
two clauses are semantically close, but semantic proximity does not establish that one
clause supplies the legal basis for another, that an exception defeats the default rule, or
that a conclusion follows only after several prior conditions are satisfied. Legal reasoning
is therefore not only a semantic task; it is a dependency-modelling task. The relevant
question is often not whether a passage is about the same topic as the query, but whether
it has the correct legal function within the reasoning chain.
The same issue appears in argument-aware legal NLP. Ali et al., 2023 show that argument extraction from judicial material remains diﬀicult even with carefully engineered con-

straints, while Elaraby and Litman, 2022 show that argument-role information improves
downstream summarisation of legal opinions. These findings matter for the present thesis
because they expose a limitation of flat text processing: if the system loses the distinction
between legal claim, legal basis, exception, application, and conclusion, it may produce
a fluent answer while distorting the reasoning structure that makes the answer legally
usable.

1.2.2

Intertextuality, Temporality, and Structural Dependency

Legal corpora are not collections of independent documents. They are networks of
typed relations. A provision may cite another article, depend on a definition located
elsewhere, incorporate a procedural rule from a different instrument, or be altered by
a later amendment. Hierarchy also matters: books, titles, chapters, sections, articles,
paragraphs, and annexes do not merely organise the document visually; they distribute
legal force across a structured system. For this reason, legal interpretation often requires
movement through a corpus rather than reading a retrieved passage in isolation.
This intertextual structure explains why graph-based representations are attractive
in legal AI. Chen et al., 2025 show that law-article recommendation improves when articles and cases are represented through a legal knowledge graph rather than through text
similarity alone. Similarly, Li et al., 2024b and d’Amato et al., 2025 treat legal graph
construction as a way of making legal relations explicit and queryable. In the Algerian
context, Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024 make the same point at the level of legal infrastructure: before reasoning over Algerian legislation can be reliable, the corpus itself must
be structured, linked, and computationally navigable.
The temporal dimension is equally important. Law changes over time: provisions are
enacted, amended, repealed, suspended, consolidated, or replaced. A legally valid answer
may therefore depend not only on which article is retrieved, but on which version of that
article was in force at the relevant date. A system that retrieves a semantically relevant
but outdated provision has not merely made a retrieval error; it has produced a legally
anachronistic answer. Colombo et al., 2025 frame this problem directly as the retrieval of
the correct version of a law for a desired timestamp, while Küçük and Can, 2025 identify
temporal modelling as a critical open issue in legal knowledge graphs.
These two dimensions—intertextuality and temporality—define a central constraint
for legal AI: legal evidence must be both structurally complete and temporally valid. A
passage that omits the controlling exception may be topically relevant but legally insuﬀicient. A provision that was amended after the event in question may be textually correct
but legally inapplicable. This is why a legal reasoning system requires more than passage
retrieval. It requires identifiable provisions, resolvable cross-references, amendment-aware

metadata, and mechanisms for checking whether the cited authority actually supports the
generated claim.

1.2.3

Legal Questions as Heterogeneous Computational Tasks

Legal questions also differ in the type of operation they require. Some questions
are close to factual lookup: the system must identify the relevant provision and return
its content accurately. Others require relational understanding: the answer is distributed
across several provisions that jointly define a legal regime. More diﬀicult questions require
compositional reasoning, where a rule, definition, exception, amendment, and factual
condition must be assembled before a conclusion can be justified. The diﬀiculty of legal
AI therefore lies not only in the structure of the corpus, but also in the diversity of the
question types imposed on the system.
This distinction is central to legal evaluation. Louis et al., 2023 show that longform legal question answering often requires answers to be constructed from multiple
provisions rather than extracted from a single passage. Zheng et al., 2025 similarly argue
for reasoning-focused legal retrieval benchmarks that test whether the retrieved material is
suﬀicient for downstream legal reasoning, not only whether it is semantically related to the

1.2.4

The Low-Resource Condition in Algerian Legal AI

In legal AI, “low-resource” is broader than the absence of annotated datasets or pretrained models. It also includes weak digitisation, inconsistent document formats, limited
legal markup, scarce jurisdiction-specific benchmarks, and insuﬀicient expert validation.
A jurisdiction may possess a large body of legal texts and still be computationally lowresource if those texts are locked in scanned Portable Document Format (PDF) files,
lack stable identifiers, omit machine-readable amendment metadata, or cannot be aligned
reliably at article level. In such a setting, the first research problem is not model optimisation but the construction of the conditions under which meaningful evaluation becomes
possible.
Algerian law is a particularly demanding case. It operates in a legal environment
shaped by Arabic, French, civil-law inheritance, Islamic legal influence, and postindependence codification. This plurality is not a superficial translation problem. It
affects terminology, legal drafting, conceptual equivalence, and the mapping between
user queries and authoritative sources. At the same time, much of the legal corpus is
available through the Journal Oﬀiciel, but availability is not the same as computational
usability. If article hierarchy, cross-references, amendment chains, and temporal validity
are not encoded in a machine-readable form, the system must infer legal structure from

Chapter 1. Artificial Intelligence & Legal Reasoning: Landscape & Limits

9

layout and surface text each time it retrieves evidence.
This makes corpus representation an infrastructural prerequisite. The Algerian legal
corpus must be transformed from publication-oriented documents into structured legal
objects before retrieval, graph construction, citation checking, or reasoning evaluation can
be reliable. Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024 are important precisely because they treat the
Algerian Oﬀicial Journal not merely as text to be searched, but as legal material that must
be extracted, normalised, structured, and linked. This thesis follows the same premise:
Algerian legal AI cannot assume that the corpus, graph, benchmark, and validation layer
already exist. They are part of the research contribution.
The resource gap is also human. Legal AI requires legal expertise to define tasks,
annotate provisions, resolve ambiguity, and judge whether an output is legally sound.
Many relevant errors are invisible to purely automatic metrics: a system may retrieve a
related provision, generate a coherent explanation, or achieve lexical overlap with a reference answer while ignoring a controlling exception or citing a non-dispositive article. In
low-resource legal settings, this expert-validation bottleneck limits not only dataset construction but also epistemic confidence in reported progress. Automated evaluation can
measure retrieval, citation overlap, and support proxies; it cannot fully replace juridical
assessment.

1.2.5

Synthesis: Consequences for System Design

The legal domain imposes a stricter design standard than ordinary open-domain question answering. A system must retrieve relevant text, but also preserve legal structure.
It must cite sources, but also ensure that the cited provisions exist and support the
claims attached to them. It must answer when the corpus contains adequate authority,
but abstain when the question imports unsupported foreign concepts or falls outside the
available legal base. It must handle semantic variation, but without confusing topical
similarity with legal suﬀiciency. It must also remain sensitive to versioning, because the
legally valid text may be date-dependent.
These requirements motivate an architecture that combines several layers rather than
relying on a single model class. Structured legal documents provide stable legal units.
Retrieval gives access to candidate evidence. Knowledge graphs make cross-references,
amendment relations, and legal dependencies traversable. Reasoning orchestration coordinates decomposition, evidence inspection, and path revision. Argument-aware evidence
structuring helps preserve the distinction between legal claim, ground, exception, and conclusion. Benchmark design then tests whether these components improve the behaviours
that matter: article-level citation, claim support, abstention, and resistance to jurisdictional contamination.

1.3

The Failure of Current AI in Law

Section 1.2 established that legal text is not generic prose. It is a structured domain shaped by conditional rules, cross-references, temporal evolution, and justificatory
demands that exceed ordinary semantic matching. The present section turns from the
nature of legal information to the limitations of current AI systems when confronted with
that structure. Its guiding claim is simple: the persistent weaknesses of legal AI do not
stem from a lack of progress, but from a mismatch between what these systems are designed to optimize and what legal reasoning requires. Each generation of methods has
solved one diﬀiculty while leaving a deeper one unresolved. Understanding this pattern
is essential for explaining why more integrated architectures have become necessary.

1.3.1

The Hallucination Problem: When Fluency Becomes a
Legal Liability

Mata v. Avianca, Inc., 2023 offer one of the clearest illustrations of the problem. In
that case, legal submissions relied on judicial decisions that did not in fact exist, but
had been generated by ChatGPT and accepted without proper verification. What made
the episode so significant was not merely that the system was wrong. It was that it was
wrong in a way that remained fluent, confident, and institutionally plausible. In ordinary
NLP tasks, such behaviour may appear as a tolerable error of generation. In law, it is
a direct threat to professional trust, because fabricated citations and unsupported legal
propositions are not minor inaccuracies; they undermine the very basis on which legal
reasoning is accepted as valid.
Ji et al., 2023 provide the theoretical vocabulary for understanding this failure through
their distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic hallucination. The distinction is especially
useful in legal settings. An intrinsic hallucination misstates the content of a source that
has actually been retrieved. An extrinsic hallucination goes further by introducing claims,
citations, or authorities that cannot be verified against any relevant source at all. Both are
damaging, but the second is particularly serious in law because it creates the appearance
of authority where none exists. A fabricated case reference, a nonexistent article number,

Chapter 1. Artificial Intelligence & Legal Reasoning: Landscape & Limits

11

or an invented amendment can survive casual reading precisely because legal discourse is
already highly formalized.
The problem is compounded by instability. Blair-Stanek and Van Durme, 2025 show
that even leading general-purpose models may produce different answers to repeated legal
queries under near-identical conditions. This is not simply a matter of stylistic variation.
In legal contexts, answer variability means that the system does not possess a stable
relation to the norm it purports to explain. A conclusion that shifts under re-prompting
is not merely inconvenient; it is jurisprudentially weak, because legal reasoning must be
contestable, repeatable, and anchored in a standard that does not fluctuate with sampling
behaviour.
Scaling alone does not eliminate this diﬀiculty. OpenAI et al., 2024 and Sengupta
et al., 2023 represent the frontier of general and Arabic-oriented large language models
respectively, yet neither class of system resolves the core problem. Larger models are often
more fluent and sometimes more useful, but fluency is not equivalent to legal reliability. In
high-stakes legal settings, a model that sounds authoritative while remaining probabilistic
in its commitment to fact is still fundamentally unsafe. What law exposes, therefore, is
not a peripheral weakness of language models but a limit of generation-first architectures
themselves.

1.3.2

The Attribution Gap:

Generating Answers Without

Grounding Them
If hallucination is the most visible failure of current AI in law, lack of attribution
is the more structural one. A legal answer is not valid merely because it is correct in
substance; it must also be traceable to an identifiable source and justifiable from that
source. Attribution is therefore not a cosmetic enhancement added after generation. It
is part of what makes a legal answer legally usable. A system that cannot show which
authority supports which claim remains epistemically opaque, even when its conclusion
happens to be right.
Rashkin et al., 2023 clarify this point by defining what it means for a statement
to be attributable to identified sources. Their framework is important because it shifts
the evaluative question away from general factuality and toward source-supported claim
making. In law, this distinction is decisive. A statement may be plausible, even accurate,
and still be unacceptable if the system cannot indicate the specific provision, case, or
document from which the claim is derived. Legal accountability requires not just answer
production, but answer provenance.
Bohnet et al., 2023 and Min et al., 2023 show why this remains diﬀicult even in
systems explicitly designed to improve factual reliability. Models trained to generate

Chapter 1. Artificial Intelligence & Legal Reasoning: Landscape & Limits

12

answers together with supporting evidence still struggle to align claims precisely with
appropriate sources, while fact-level evaluation reveals that strong LLM outputs continue
to contain a non-trivial share of unverifiable statements. These findings matter because
they expose a persistent gap between textual support and inferential support. A system
may retrieve evidence and still fail to connect the right part of that evidence to the right
part of the answer.
Recent attempts to repair this failure are revealing. Gao et al., 2023a propose RARR
as a post-hoc revision pipeline that retrieves evidence and edits model outputs to improve
factual grounding. This is a meaningful advance, but its very design exposes the limitation
it tries to solve: attribution is added after the initial reasoning has already been produced.
The answer first emerges from a generative process that is not natively grounded, and only
afterwards is it revised toward support. In law, this sequencing is problematic, because
justification is not something that should be retrofitted once a conclusion exists; it is part
of the reasoning path by which that conclusion should be reached.
This problem persists even in law-specific settings. Zhang et al., 2025b show through
CitaLaw that citation-aware legal generation can reduce unsupported outputs, yet
citation-answer consistency remains fragile, especially when the answer synthesizes across
multiple provisions. This is an important reminder that inserting citations into legal
text is not the same as reasoning from law. The attribution gap is therefore deeper than
missing references. It is the gap between producing an answer that is accompanied by
sources and producing an answer whose inferential structure is genuinely governed by
those sources.

1.3.3

Retrieval at Its Limits: From Vocabulary Mismatch to
Structural Blindness

Retrieval has substantially improved legal AI, but its progress also reveals a persistent
boundary. Lexical methods such as BM25 remain strong for exact article numbers, law
identifiers, and technical legal terms (Robertson and Zaragoza, 2009), while dense retrieval
reduces vocabulary mismatch by recovering semantically related passages even when the
query and provision use different wording (Karpukhin et al., 2020). Late-interaction,
neural-sparse, and hybrid methods further improve ranking by combining fine-grained
matching and complementary retrieval signals (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020, Formal et al.,
2021b, Cormack et al., 2009). Yet these improvements do not remove the central legal limitation: retrieval ranks text, whereas legal reasoning often depends on relations between
texts.
This limitation is structural rather than merely empirical. A retrieved article may be
topically relevant but legally incomplete if it omits a definition, exception, cross-reference,

Chapter 1. Artificial Intelligence & Legal Reasoning: Landscape & Limits

13

amendment, or temporal condition that controls its interpretation. Graph-oriented retrieval therefore represents an important step because it treats relations between legal
units as part of the retrieval problem rather than as incidental metadata (Edge et al.,
2025). However, even graph-aware retrieval does not by itself guarantee legal suﬀiciency.
As recent studies show, legal RAG systems still fail through predictable patterns: dangling
references, incomplete evidence chains, reasoning truncation, and temporal mismatch between the retrieved provision and the legally applicable version (Kim and Lee, 2025, Zheng
et al., 2025). The problem is therefore not simply finding relevant text, but retrieving the
set of sources required for a legally valid answer.

1.3.4

Multi-Hop and Compositional Reasoning: Where SingleStep Systems Break

Legal conclusions rarely follow from one passage alone. They often require a chain
of operations: locating the applicable rule, resolving a definition, checking an exception,
following a cross-reference, verifying the relevant version, and then explaining how the
evidence supports the conclusion. This makes legal reasoning more demanding than ordinary multi-hop QA. The dependency between steps is not merely factual but normative:
one provision may control, limit, defeat, or temporally replace another.
General multi-hop and iterative retrieval methods respond to part of this diﬀiculty
by decomposing complex questions and gathering evidence across several steps (Qi et al.,
2019, Qi et al., 2021, Press et al., 2023, Khot et al., 2022). Recent RAG variants add
further control by deciding when retrieval is needed, whether retrieved evidence is relevant,
and whether generated claims are supported (Asai et al., 2023, Wang et al., 2025). These
methods improve evidentiary discipline, but they do not inherently model the legal force
that connects provisions, exceptions, amendments, and citations.
This is where recursive control becomes relevant. RLMs treat long contexts as external environments that can be inspected, decomposed, and revisited rather than consumed
once as a flat prompt (?). For legal AI, the importance of this idea is not merely longer
context, but orchestration: the system can delay final generation while it checks whether
the evidence chain is complete. Legal-specific work also points in this direction, although
existing frameworks remain tied to better-resourced legal traditions and do not transfer
directly to Arabic civil-law corpora such as Algerian legislation (Song et al., 2026). The
unresolved issue is therefore one of reasoning control: legal AI needs mechanisms that coordinate retrieval, structural expansion, evidence inspection, and support checking before
producing a citation-faithful answer.

Domain and Language Shift: The Non-English Legal AI
Gap

Many of the methods discussed so far were developed and benchmarked in English legal
environments with comparatively mature digital infrastructure. This matters because
their apparent success can obscure how sharply performance and reliability deteriorate
once the system is moved across languages, jurisdictions, or legal traditions. The nonEnglish problem in legal AI is not just an issue of translation. It is a domain shift affecting
corpora, concepts, drafting conventions, citation practices, available benchmarks, and the
very structure of legal sources.
Ariai et al., 2024 and Küçük and Can, 2025 make this imbalance visible at the survey
level. The field remains heavily concentrated around English-language resources, especially U.S. and European materials, with far fewer benchmarks, ontologies, and validated
pipelines for lower-resource jurisdictions. What appears as a general legal-AI method is
often, in practice, a method tailored to a narrow subset of legal ecosystems. Once this
narrowness is acknowledged, the diﬀiculty of transfer becomes easier to understand: systems trained on abundant English case law and richly structured digital collections are
poorly calibrated for jurisdictions where corpora are thinner, formats less standardized,
and expert evaluation harder to organize.
Arabic legal AI illustrates this clearly. Hijazi et al., 2024 and Abu Shairah et al., 2025
show that Arabic legal benchmarking is only beginning to emerge, and that current models
remain substantially less reliable than in English settings. Arabic-specific pretraining
helps, as shown by AL-Qurishi et al., 2022, while broader Arabic NLP efforts such as
Antoun et al., 2020 provide important groundwork. Even so, the gap remains pronounced.
Legal Arabic is morphologically complex, terminologically dense, and underrepresented in
the large-scale legal datasets that shaped the dominant trajectory of legal NLP. Sengupta
et al., 2023 extend capability in Arabic-oriented LLMs, but they do not dissolve the
structural scarcity of legal-domain resources.
The Algerian case intensifies this pattern. Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024 show that
even building a structured representation of Algerian legislation is already a substantive
research contribution. This is a decisive point. In many English-language settings, the
corpus is already digitized, segmented, and queryable, so the research focus can begin
directly with retrieval or reasoning. In Algerian law, by contrast, representation itself
becomes a first-order problem. The pipeline cannot start from inference because it must
first make the corpus computationally legible. This means that the Algerian setting is
not simply one more application domain among others. It is a revealing test case for the
limits of a field that has often assumed that the necessary infrastructure already exists.
Even multilingual legal pretraining does not fully solve this problem. Niklaus et al.,

2024 broaden the multilingual horizon, but Arabic legal material remains far less central
than English, and Algerian legislation remains especially marginal. The consequence is not
merely lower benchmark performance. It is a compounding of failure modes: hallucination
becomes harder to detect, retrieval becomes less stable across languages, reasoning chains
become more fragile, and expert validation becomes more diﬀicult to sustain. The nonEnglish gap is therefore not peripheral to legal AI. It is one of the clearest places where
the limits of current systems become visible.

1.3.6

Synthesis: What Partial Solutions Collectively Reveal

Taken together, the literature reviewed in this section reveals a clear path. Successive
generations of AI systems have addressed genuine weaknesses in their predecessors: lexical
retrieval improved upon Boolean matching, dense retrieval reduced vocabulary mismatch,
hybrid pipelines increased recall, graph-based methods recovered part of legal structure,
and recent RAG variants introduced reflection, verification, and adaptive control. Yet
each improvement has tended to repair one symptom while leaving the deeper legal problem intact. Fluency does not prevent hallucination. Retrieval does not guarantee legal
suﬀiciency. Citation does not ensure justification. More context does not automatically
yield valid reasoning.
Kim and Lee, 2025, Blair-Stanek and Van Durme, 2025, and Guha et al., 2023 collectively make this point from different angles. Well-engineered pipelines still fail on
structurally legal tasks, leading models remain unstable under repeated querying, and
benchmark success does not generalize into a unified form of legal competence. At the
survey level, Ariai et al., 2024 confirm that the field remains fragmented across tasks,
languages, and evaluation regimes. What emerges from these partial solutions is therefore not the image of a nearly solved problem, but of a domain that exposes the limits of
isolated paradigms.
The central implication for this thesis is that legal AI needs a more integrated response.
Reliable systems must combine grounded retrieval, explicit modelling of legal structure,
and forms of reasoning whose inferential path is inspectable rather than opaque. In
low-resource jurisdictions such as Algerian law, they must do so while also confronting
linguistic and infrastructural scarcity. The next chapter turns from these failures to the
foundational pillars on which such an architecture can be built.

From Diagnosis to Research Direction

1.4.1

The Research Gaps

16

This review has identified several gaps that remain insuﬀiciently addressed in the
current literature and that are especially acute in the Algerian context.
The first gap is the absence of a structured Algerian legal corpus. Existing Algerian
legal material is available, but not in a form that directly supports retrieval, graph construction, citation resolution, or reasoning-oriented analysis. Without a machine-readable
corpus in which provisions are uniquely identified, hierarchically organised, and linked
through resolvable references, higher-level legal AI remains severely constrained. In such
a setting, the corpus is not just a data source for generation; it is itself one necessary
condition for reliable legal reasoning.
The second gap is the absence of a temporally-aware legal knowledge graph for Algerian law. Legal graphs in the literature already show the value of structured legal
representation, but temporality remains one of the field’s most persistent open problems.
In the Algerian case, this challenge is magnified by multilingual drafting, long amendment histories, and the lack of structured metadata that would make version tracking
straightforward. A graph that represents provisions, citations, and legal entities without
representing their evolution over time can improve access to legal structure, but it cannot
fully guarantee that the retrieved norm is the legally applicable one.
The third gap is the absence of recursive reasoning orchestration for low-resource legal
AI. Existing retrieval, graph-based, and verification-oriented approaches address important parts of the legal reasoning problem, but they are often treated as separate mechanisms rather than as components of a controlled reasoning loop. Many legal questions
require the system to decompose a query, retrieve relevant provisions, follow structural
links, identify missing evidence, revise the search path, and only then synthesize a legally
justified answer. This creates a need for a Recursive Language Model (RLM) layer able to
coordinate retrieval, graph traversal, evidence selection, support checking, and generation,
rather than relying on a single-pass retrieve-then-generate pipeline.
The fourth gap concerns evaluation. Existing legal AI benchmarks have advanced the
field considerably, but none is tailored to the specific demands of Algerian law. There is
currently no benchmark designed to evaluate bilingual legal retrieval, temporally sensitive
reasoning, amendment-chain handling, citation faithfulness, hallucination control, and
abstention behaviour within this jurisdiction. As a result, system performance cannot yet
be assessed against a domain-specific standard that reflects the forms of legal reliability
required in actual use.
The fifth gap concerns argument-aware evidence structuring for Algerian legal reason-

Chapter 1. Artificial Intelligence & Legal Reasoning: Landscape & Limits

17

ing. While legal argument mining has been studied in other jurisdictions, no annotated
corpus currently exists for Algerian legal texts in either Arabic or French. However,
the practical requirement of the present thesis is narrower than full supervised argument
mining. The central need is to organise retrieved legal material into lightweight argumentaware units, such as ADU-inspired triplets, that expose claims, legal bases, conditions,
exceptions, and support relations before generation. This reframing preserves the value of
argumentation theory for explainability, while avoiding the stronger claim that the thesis
builds a complete argument-mining system for Algerian law.
The sixth gap is the integration gap. Retrieval, legal knowledge graphs, recursive
reasoning control, citation verification, and argument-aware evidence structuring have
each been explored to different degrees in the literature, and some pairwise combinations
already exist. However, no unified architecture has been proposed for a low-resource
Arabic legal system that integrates these components into a single pipeline for citationfaithful and explainable legal reasoning. The challenge is therefore not only to improve
each component individually, but to make them interoperate within a coherent framework
in which legal evidence is retrieved, structurally expanded, recursively examined, and
finally transformed into an answer whose claims remain traceable to authoritative sources.

2.2

The Arabic Legal Corpus Problem: Infrastructure as a Precondition

The first foundation of legal AI in the Algerian setting is not a retrieval model, a graph
19

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

20

database, or an argumentation framework, but the legal corpus itself. This is because the
practical diﬀiculty of Algerian legal data is not exhausted by language, size, or scarcity.
It lies more deeply in the fact that the corpus is available primarily as documents rather
than as structured legal objects. The central question of this section is therefore not what
has already been built, but what the correct standard must be if downstream reasoning
is to be reliable at all.
Algerian legal texts are often available as PDF, including scanned reproductions of
older oﬀicial publications, and this format is far more consequential than it first appears.
A statute is not merely a sequence of sentences distributed across pages; it is a hierarchical object composed of books, titles, chapters, sections, subsections, and articles, each
carrying part of the legal organisation of the text. In a PDF, this hierarchy is flattened
into visual layout. What the reader can infer typographically, the machine must reconstruct from scratch. The same loss affects legal cross-references. When one provision
invokes another, the reference appears only as ordinary text, not as a resolvable pointer
to an identifiable target. The result is that a core feature of legal meaning — dependence
on other provisions — becomes invisible to computation. Küçük and Can, 2025 make
clear that this problem is not marginal but central to computational law: without explicit
structure, legal documents remain far more diﬀicult to search, link, and reason over than
their textual availability suggests.
The same structural loss extends to metadata and amendment history. A legal text
published as a PDF rarely provides machine-readable information about enactment date,
effective date, repeal, or amendment status, even though these are often indispensable to
legal interpretation. In practice, this means that a provision may be textually accessible
while its temporal validity remains opaque. The problem is sharper still when amendments
are involved. Oﬀicial publications may show an amended version or an earlier one, but they
rarely preserve both in a form that explicitly identifies what changed, when it changed,
and which version was in force at a given moment. For legal research, this is already
burdensome; for legal AI, it is disabling. Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024 show why the
Algerian context makes this especially serious: even the task of structuring and linking
legal material is already a substantive research problem, which means that inference
cannot begin from a ready-made legal database but must first confront the prior diﬀiculty
of corpus representation.
The appropriate response to this problem is not ad hoc text cleaning, but a legal
document model designed for machine readability from the outset. This is the role of
Akoma Ntoso, the LegalDocML standard maintained by OASIS. OASIS LegalDocML TC,
2018 define a representation in which provisions are encoded through explicit hierarchy,
resolvable references, temporal metadata, unique identifiers, and semantic annotations.
In such a model, a provision is no longer just a text fragment, but a uniquely identifiable
legal unit whose links and lifecycle can be processed directly. This is not simply a matter

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

21

of markup. It is the condition on which retrieval, knowledge graph construction, and
argument mining all depend.
The real contrast, therefore, is not between PDF and eXtensible Markup Language
(XML) as formats, but between two states of the corpus. In one, hierarchy, references,
dates, and amendment chains remain implicit and must be reconstructed repeatedly by
downstream components. In the other, they are already encoded as first-class legal objects. Niklaus et al., 2024 make clear that even large multilingual legal corpora remain
insuﬀicient for structure-aware reasoning if they provide only text without explicit legal
linkage. In legal AI, raw text is therefore not the same as usable legal data.
The diﬀiculty is that conversion to this standard is itself a demanding pipeline rather
than a one-time formatting step. It requires Optical Character Recognition (OCR)
adapted to legal layouts, structure extraction, entity and relation extraction, and expert validation. Küçük and Can, 2025 note that even well-resourced jurisdictions struggle
to achieve reliable conversion at scale. In the Algerian setting, fully automatic perfection
is not realistic. What matters instead is reaching a suﬀiciently high level of structured
accuracy while ensuring that the remaining errors are visible and subject to expert review
rather than silently propagated.
This infrastructural weakness has consequences beyond document conversion itself.
Without structured legal corpora, downstream tasks remain underdeveloped: training
data for legal Named Entity Recognition (NER) and relation extraction remain scarce,
knowledge graph construction becomes unreliable, and benchmark design becomes substantially harder. This is especially serious in Arabic legal AI, where evaluation resources
are already limited, and in Algerian law, where jurisdiction-specific benchmarks are almost
entirely absent. The result is that progress is often claimed at the model level without
the underlying corpus and evaluation conditions needed to measure legal reliability convincingly.
A related diﬀiculty is the scarcity of expert validation. Ariai et al., 2024 report that
only 23% of 147 legal NLP papers surveyed included validation by legal professionals.
This figure is especially important in the present context because the corpus problem is
not merely technical. Recovering the intended structure of a legal document, checking
whether a cross-reference has been resolved correctly, or determining whether an extracted
amendment relation is legally valid are all tasks that ultimately require juridical judgment.

2.3

Information Retrieval and Retrieval-Augmented
Generation

Once legal documents have been converted into structured legal objects, the next problem is evidence access. A citation-faithful legal system must identify the provisions that
may support an answer before any generation step occurs. In the architecture developed in
this thesis, IR provides this evidentiary entry point, while RAG conditions generation on
retrieved legal material rather than on parametric memory alone. Their role is therefore
not merely to improve answer fluency, but to constrain the answer space to identifiable
legal sources.
The legal setting makes retrieval unusually demanding. Legal queries frequently con-

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

23

tain exact identifiers—article numbers, law numbers, dates, institutional names, or technical terms—for which lexical matching remains indispensable. At the same time, users
may formulate questions through paraphrase, lay language, Arabic orthographic variants, French terminology, or mixed legal expressions, which creates vocabulary mismatch.
Retrieval for legal reasoning must therefore balance two requirements that are often in
tension: exactness for legal identifiers and semantic flexibility for concept-level access.
The literature on lexical, dense, late-interaction, neural-sparse, and hybrid retrieval can
be read as a sequence of increasingly sophisticated attempts to manage this tension.

2.3.1

Retrieval Paradigms and Their Legal Trade-Offs

Lexical retrieval remains a strong baseline because law is citation-heavy. Methods
such as Best Match 25 (probabilistic retrieval model) (BM25) are well suited to queries
containing article references, law numbers, named institutions, and stable doctrinal terms
(Robertson and Zaragoza, 2009). Their transparency is also valuable in legal settings:
a system designer can inspect why a document was retrieved and identify which terms
drove the match. The limitation is equally clear. Lexical retrieval depends on surface
overlap. It is vulnerable to Arabic morphology, orthographic variation, legal synonymy,
and bilingual mismatch, all of which are present in Algerian legal material.
Dense retrieval addresses part of this weakness by embedding queries and documents
in a shared semantic space. Dual-encoder models such as DPR improve recall when
the query and the relevant provision express the same concept using different wording
(Karpukhin et al., 2020). This is essential for layman questions and concept-oriented legal
queries. However, dense similarity is not legal suﬀiciency. A semantically close provision
may fail to contain the applicable exception, the controlling definition, or the relevant
amended version. For legal AI, this is a decisive limitation: the nearest passage is not
necessarily the legally complete evidence set.
Late-interaction retrieval, exemplified by ColBERT, attempts to recover some of the
precision lost in dense retrieval by preserving token-level interactions between query and
document representations (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020). This makes it attractive where
both semantic flexibility and fine-grained legal terminology matter. Its cost is operational:
indexing and storage requirements are heavier, which may be diﬀicult to justify in a small
or evolving corpus unless the precision gain is empirically demonstrated. Neural sparse
retrieval, represented by SPLADE, offers a different compromise: it retains sparse indexing
while learning semantic expansion over the vocabulary (Formal et al., 2021b; Formal et al.,
2021a). This can recover terms absent from the original query, but its reliability depends
on whether the underlying model has learned the relevant legal Arabic, French, and
jurisdiction-specific vocabulary.

Hybrid retrieval is the practical response to the fact that these methods fail in different
ways. Sparse methods preserve legal identifiers; dense methods improve semantic recall;
late-interaction methods refine term-level alignment; neural sparse methods bridge lexical
search and learned expansion. Fusion methods such as Reciprocal Rank Fusion combine
ranked lists without requiring all retrieval channels to share the same score scale (Cormack
et al., 2009). In legal applications, the point of hybrid retrieval is not methodological
eclecticism; it is risk control. The system should not lose an exact article reference because
a dense model prefers a paraphrastic neighbour, nor should it miss a conceptually relevant
provision because the query lacks the exact legal wording.

2.3.2

Retrieval-Augmented Generation as Grounded but Incomplete Reasoning

RAG changes the role of retrieval by making retrieved evidence part of the generation
process. Instead of relying only on what the language model stores in its parameters, the
generator is conditioned on external documents that can be updated, inspected, and cited
(Lewis et al., 2021). In legal contexts, this shift is fundamental. It reduces dependence on
unsupported model memory and creates the possibility of answers grounded in identifiable
legal sources.
However, legal RAG should not be confused with legal reasoning. A retrieve-thengenerate pipeline can still fail in several ways. It may retrieve the right document but
the wrong article. It may retrieve a relevant article while omitting the definition or
exception that controls its interpretation. It may cite a real provision but infer a rule that
the provision does not support. It may also answer an unanswerable or jurisdictionally
infected question by importing material from another legal system. These failures are not
solved by placing retrieved passages in the prompt; they require verification, structural
constraints, and abstention mechanisms.
Subsequent RAG architectures can be understood as attempts to repair specific weaknesses in the basic retrieve-then-generate pattern. Fusion-in-Decoder increases the model’s
ability to use several retrieved passages, which matters when a legal answer depends on
multiple provisions rather than a single article (Izacard and Grave, 2021). RETRO shows
that external memory can complement parametric capacity at scale (Borgeaud et al.,
2022). These systems improve the use of retrieved evidence, but they do not by themselves determine whether the evidence set is legally complete.
A second line of work introduces explicit support checking and correction. Self-RAG
trains the model to decide when retrieval is needed, whether retrieved evidence is relevant,
and whether generated claims are supported (Asai et al., 2023). Corrective RAG evaluates retrieval quality and triggers repair when the evidence is weak (Yan et al., 2024).
Speculative RAG separates drafting from verification to improve eﬀiciency while retaining
evidentiary control (Wang et al., 2025). These approaches are particularly relevant to
legal AI because they treat grounding as an active control process rather than a passive
context-insertion step.
A third line improves query preparation and iterative evidence acquisition. HyDE uses
a hypothetical document to bridge the gap between short or underspecified queries and the
more formal wording of target passages (Gao et al., 2023b), while Query2Doc expands
the query with pseudo-document context (Wang et al., 2023). Iterative retrieval and

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

26

decomposition methods similarly break complex questions into smaller evidence-seeking
steps (Qi et al., 2019; Qi et al., 2021). These methods are directly relevant to statutory
reasoning, where a complete answer may require the rule, the definition, the exception,
and the amendment history to be collected before synthesis.
The resulting lesson is precise. RAG is a necessary grounding layer, but it is not a
complete architecture for legal reasoning. It improves evidence access and reduces hallucination risk, but it does not inherently model legal hierarchy, cross-reference structure,
amendment history, article-level citation validity, or claim-level support. In the present
thesis, RAG is therefore positioned as one component in a larger constrained architecture.
Retrieval constructs the candidate evidence pool; the article registry determines whether
citations exist; the knowledge graph exposes structural and temporal relations; typed
handlers select the reasoning path; and faithfulness gates verify whether generated claims
are supported by the cited provisions.
This positioning is important for the transition to the next pillar. IR and RAG
improve access to legal text, but they still operate primarily over passages. They do not
by themselves make legal dependency explicit. The next section therefore turns to legal
knowledge graphs, whose role is to represent provisions, references, amendments, and legal
concepts as queryable relations rather than leaving them implicit in retrieved text.

2.4

Legal Knowledge Graphs

IR and RAG improve access to legal text, but they still operate primarily over passages. They can help locate relevant provisions and reduce hallucination, yet they do
not by themselves make explicit the structural relations that connect one provision to
another. In law, those relations are not secondary. Definitions, amendments, citations,
abrogations, and hierarchical containment often determine how a provision should be interpreted. This is why a legal knowledge graph is needed. Its purpose is to transform
these dependencies into explicit and queryable relations, so that legal structure becomes
available to computation rather than remaining hidden in text alone.

2.4.1

Ontological and Construction Foundations

A legal knowledge graph requires more than extracted entities and relations. It also
needs a clear model of what counts as a legal object and of the kinds of relations that
may connect such objects. This is the role of ontology. Hoekstra et al., 2007 address this
need through the Legal Knowledge Interchange Format (LKIF), which provides a formal
vocabulary for concepts such as norms, roles, obligations, permissions, and legal acts. Its

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

27

main value lies in expressiveness: it represents legal knowledge in a form that is closer
to legal reasoning than ordinary information graphs. Its limitation is practical. A rich
ontology is diﬀicult to populate at scale, especially when the source corpus is noisy and
only partially structured.
A second requirement concerns document identity across time. In legal corpora, a
law is not a single static text. It may pass through several amended versions while
remaining the same legal work. Küçük and Can, 2025 discuss this problem through the
Archetypes and Links Ontology for Legal Texts (ALLOT), which distinguishes the legal
work, its textual expression, and its digital manifestation. This distinction is essential for
versioning. Without it, the graph cannot reliably represent the difference between a law
as a legal object and one of its amended textual states.
The most directly relevant foundation here is Akoma Ntoso. OASIS LegalDocML TC,
2018 define it as a legal XML standard that encodes hierarchy, references, identifiers, and
temporal metadata in machine-readable form. This connects the graph directly to the
corpus problem discussed earlier in the chapter. A provision encoded in Akoma Ntoso
is no longer just a block of text: it has an identity, a position in the hierarchy, explicit
references, and a lifecycle. Akoma Ntoso is therefore not simply a document format. It
is the structural basis that makes graph construction possible.
Once this basis exists, the next challenge is population. A legal graph must be filled
with entities, provisions, and relations extracted from the corpus. Li et al., 2024b show
one important direction through LLM-assisted graph construction over Chinese civil law.
Their contribution is useful because it shows that graph construction is not only a representational improvement, but also a reasoning improvement: when legal relations are made
explicit, downstream question answering becomes more effective, especially on multi-hop
questions. The graph matters because it changes what the system can connect, not only
what it can store.
Chen et al., 2025 illustrate a second methodological point. Their graph does not rely
only on explicit citations between legal articles, but also introduces co-relevance edges
derived from patterns of co-application in court decisions. This shows that a legal graph
can represent not only formal textual links, but also expert patterns of legal use. In other
words, graph construction can capture how provisions are used together in practice, not
only how they cite each other on paper.
d’Amato et al., 2025 clarify the construction problem further by distinguishing bottomup and top-down strategies. Bottom-up pipelines extract entities and relations directly
from documents, whereas top-down approaches begin from a predefined schema and populate it in a more guided way. Their work shows that the appropriate choice depends on
the corpus and the objective: highly structured sources favour bottom-up precision, while
noisier or more exploratory settings often require top-down guidance. This distinction is
directly relevant here, because the Algerian corpus is neither fully structured nor fully

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

28

homogeneous.
The closest precedent for this thesis is Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024, who construct
a legal knowledge graph from the Journal Oﬀiciel de la République Algérienne in Arabic
and French. Their work matters for two reasons. First, it shows that Algerian legal graph
construction is feasible. Second, it makes the diﬀiculty of the task visible by documenting
the performance gap between French and Arabic extraction. That gap is not a minor
technical issue. It shows that graph construction in the Algerian context is constrained
by the same resource and language limitations identified in earlier sections.

2.4.2

Query Capabilities, GraphRAG, and Temporal Limits

The value of a legal knowledge graph becomes clearest when one asks what it enables
that retrieval alone does not. A retriever ranks passages by lexical or semantic similarity.
A graph, by contrast, allows the system to query explicit legal relations. This makes
certain legal questions much easier to formulate computationally. A system can ask which
articles define a concept, which provisions amend a given article, which laws cite the same
legal basis, or which amendment chain was in force at a given date. These are not simply
richer retrieval operations. They are structurally different operations because they rely
on explicit relations rather than on text similarity alone.
This advantage becomes even more important for multi-hop reasoning. In legal corpora, the relevant answer may depend on a chain of linked provisions: one article defines
a term, another sets the rule, a third introduces an exception, and a later amendment
changes the applicable version. A graph makes that chain traversable. Instead of hoping that all relevant pieces are retrieved as disconnected passages, the system can move
explicitly from one provision to another through typed relations. This is the central
contribution of the legal graph: it turns legal dependency into a queryable object.
Temporal querying provides a particularly clear example. A question such as “What
did Article 15 provide on 15 June 2015?” cannot be answered reliably by similarity search
alone. It requires version-aware traversal over amendment history. Colombo et al., 2025
show why this has implications for graph design itself. In their work, property graphs and
Cypher are especially natural for representing dated modification chains, because temporal
edges can be traversed directly. Their point is not that Resource Description Framework
(RDF) or SPARQL Protocol and RDF Query Language (SPARQL) are unusable, but
that legal temporality becomes easier to handle when time is encoded as part of the
graph structure rather than treated as an afterthought.
Ordinary graph querying already provides a major advantage over retrieval alone because it answers structured legal questions deterministically. Once provisions, amendments, definitions, and citation links are represented as explicit relations, the system

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

29

can traverse them directly instead of inferring them indirectly from textual similarity.
GraphRAG extends this logic into generation. Rather than using the graph only as a
backend lookup structure, it uses graph relations to guide which evidence is retrieved and
how that evidence is organised before answer generation. Edge et al., 2025 show that
graph-guided retrieval can reduce irrelevant evidence by expanding along graph structure
rather than relying only on local text similarity. Baek et al., 2023 demonstrate, through
KAPING, that structured triples can be injected into prompting so that generation is
supported not only by raw passages but also by explicit facts and relations. Sun et al.,
2023 go further by allowing large language models to reason over graph paths rather than
over isolated chunks alone. The shared contribution of these approaches is therefore clear:
the graph no longer remains a passive storage layer, but becomes an active component in
evidence selection and path-aware generation. Yet even this extension leaves one major
issue unresolved. GraphRAG can exploit structural relations once they are represented,
but it still depends on a graph whose temporal state remains accurate over time.
The literature also makes clear that one major problem remains unresolved: temporality. Küçük and Can, 2025 identify temporal modelling as one of the most critical open
issues in legal knowledge graphs. Many systems still produce a static graph of the current
law rather than a graph of legal states evolving through time. This is a serious limitation,
because a static graph becomes partially obsolete as soon as the law is amended. The
problem is not theoretical. In Algerian commercial law, provisions of the Code de commerce may exist through several amended states while remaining identifiable as the same
article. Without temporal versioning, a system may retrieve an outdated formulation for
a query about present corporate obligations, thereby returning text that is relevant in
wording but invalid in law. In legal reasoning, a graph that cannot represent change over
time cannot fully represent the law.
The methodological conclusion is therefore clear. A legal knowledge graph makes
structural dependencies explicit and queryable, and it extends retrieval by making those
dependencies available to downstream reasoning. But it still does not decide how a complex legal question should be decomposed, when retrieval should be repeated, which graph
paths should be followed, or whether the available evidence is suﬀicient for a citationfaithful answer. These operations require a reasoning-control layer that can coordinate

2.5.1

From Long-Context Scaling to Recursive Inference

The immediate motivation for RLMs comes from a limitation of long-context language
modelling. Larger context windows allow more text to be placed in the prompt, but they
do not guarantee that the model will identify the legally operative part of that text,
preserve all relevant dependencies, or reason correctly across distant provisions. In legal
corpora, this distinction is decisive. A statute, oﬀicial journal, or consolidated code may fit
within a long context window and still remain diﬀicult to use, because the relevant answer
may depend on a definition in one chapter, an exception in another, and an amendment
introduced in a later instrument. Context length increases availability; it does not by
itself impose reasoning discipline.
? formulate RLMs as an inference-time strategy for this problem. In their framework,
the long prompt is treated as part of an external environment rather than as a sequence
that must be fully absorbed in a single forward pass. The root language model can
programmatically inspect this environment, select relevant snippets, decompose the task
into sub-queries, and recursively call itself or another model on smaller pieces of the
context. This changes the nature of long-context processing. The system is not asked
to remember everything at once; it is given a mechanism for deciding what should be
examined, in what order, and at what level of granularity.
This distinction matters for legal AI. Legal reasoning is not equivalent to summarising
a long document. It is a controlled movement through a normative structure. A model
may need to locate the applicable article, resolve a cross-reference, retrieve the definition
of a term, determine whether an exception applies, and verify whether the cited version
was in force at the relevant date. A flat long-context prompt leaves this control implicit.
Recursive inference makes it explicit: the model can decompose the problem, delegate
smaller evidence checks, collect intermediate results, and delay final synthesis until the
evidentiary path is more complete.

eﬀiciency perspective by separating drafting and verification. These approaches are important because they move RAG away from a passive retrieve-then-generate pipeline and
toward an active evidence-control process.

2.5.3

Legal Relevance: Normative Chains, Temporality, and Citation Faithfulness

The value of recursive orchestration becomes clearer when the legal nature of the
reasoning task is made explicit. In ordinary multi-hop QA, a reasoning chain often links
factual propositions: entity A is related to entity B, entity B is related to entity C, and

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

34

the answer follows. Legal reasoning is different. The relation between steps is normative
rather than merely factual. One provision may define the scope of another. An exception
may defeat a conclusion otherwise supported by a general rule. A later amendment
may render an older formulation inapplicable. A procedural requirement may determine
whether a substantive right can be invoked at all. These relations cannot be reduced to
semantic similarity between passages.
This is precisely where a single-pass pipeline remains fragile. A retriever may return
a provision that is topically relevant but legally incomplete. A graph may expose an
amendment chain, but the system still has to decide whether the question requires the preamendment or post-amendment version. A generator may cite a real article but infer a rule
that the article does not actually support. Legal reasoning therefore requires a sequence
of controlled operations: identify the issue, locate the candidate rule, follow structural
dependencies, check temporal validity, inspect possible exceptions, verify support, and
only then generate the answer.
RLM-based orchestration gives this sequence an architectural form. The root controller can decompose a legal question into sub-questions, such as identifying the relevant
code, locating the governing article, checking whether a definition is needed, or determining whether an amendment affects the answer. Retrieval can then be performed on
each sub-question rather than on the initial query alone. Graph traversal can expand
the candidate evidence along explicit legal relations, such as defines, amends, cites, or
createsVersion. Support checking can test whether the final claim is warranted by the
cited article text. If the evidence remains insuﬀicient, the system can revise the path
rather than forcing generation from an incomplete context.
This role is especially important for citation faithfulness. In law, citation is not merely
a reference attached to an answer after the fact. It is part of the answer’s validity. A
legal claim must be connected to the provision that supports it, and unsupported claims
must either be revised or excluded. Recursive orchestration therefore supports citation
faithfulness in two ways. First, it allows the system to gather evidence progressively,
rather than relying on whatever appears in the first retrieval set. Second, it creates
natural points at which citation existence, jurisdictional validity, and claim-level support
can be checked before the final answer is emitted.
For the Algerian context, this is not an optional refinement. The corpus is multilingual, partially structured, and historically layered. Arabic morphology, French legal
terminology, document aliases, and amendment histories all create opportunities for retrieval mismatch and citation error. In such a setting, a single-pass system is likely either
to answer from incomplete evidence or to compensate through parametric memory. The
RLM layer provides a different strategy: make insuﬀiciency visible, expand the reasoning path when justified, and prefer abstention when no corpus-grounded answer can be
supported.

Design Implications and Limits

The methodological implication is that RLM should not be treated as a substitute for
legal infrastructure. It is a control layer over that infrastructure. It still depends on a
structured corpus to define valid legal units, on retrieval to access candidate evidence, on
the knowledge graph to expose legal relations, and on argument-aware evidence structuring to organise the final reasoning trace. Without these components, recursive reasoning
would risk becoming only a more elaborate form of unsupported generation.
For this reason, the proposed framework adopts a bounded and constrained interpretation of RLM-based orchestration. Recursion is useful only if each step is anchored
in a verifiable legal operation. The system should not be allowed to generate arbitrary
chains of thought over legal material without checking whether the cited provisions exist,
whether they belong to Algerian law, whether the temporal state is correct, and whether
the generated claim is supported by the cited text. In legal AI, the goal is not maximal
autonomy, but controlled autonomy under legal constraints.
This also explains why deterministic components remain essential. Article registries,
canonical document identifiers, graph edges, amendment chains, and citation-existence
checks should not be delegated to the language model’s memory. They should be handled
by explicit data structures and verification procedures. The language model is most useful
where semantic flexibility is required: reformulating a lay query, decomposing a complex
question, summarising evidence, identifying whether a legal exception may be relevant,
and synthesising a readable answer. The deterministic infrastructure defines the legal
boundary within which that flexibility can operate.
The limitations are equally important. RLM-based orchestration does not guarantee
legal correctness on its own. Recursive systems can over-decompose a question, follow
irrelevant paths, accumulate errors across sub-calls, or increase latency and cost if recursion is not bounded. In low-resource settings, these risks are amplified because retrieval
errors and missing graph links are more likely. The correct design response is therefore
not open-ended recursion, but disciplined recursion: limited depth, explicit stopping conditions, evidence suﬀiciency checks, and safe abstention when the required support cannot
be recovered.
The contribution of RLM-based orchestration can therefore be summarised as a shift
from evidence-conditioned generation to recursively controlled legal reasoning. RAG
grounds the answer in retrieved sources; the knowledge graph makes legal relations
traversable; the RLM layer coordinates the sequence of retrieval, traversal, inspection,
verification, and synthesis; and argument-aware evidence structuring makes the final reasoning trace more explicit. In this sense, RLM is not an additional component placed
beside the other foundations, but the mechanism that allows them to operate as a coher-

ent legal reasoning system.

2.6

Argument-Aware Evidence Structuring

A legal knowledge graph makes structural dependencies explicit, but it does not by
itself explain how retrieved provisions support, qualify, or limit a legal conclusion. This is
the role of argument-aware evidence structuring in the proposed framework. The objective
is not to build a full supervised argument-mining system for Algerian law, but to organise retrieved and graph-expanded material into lightweight ADU-inspired evidence units
before generation. This makes the final answer more inspectable by separating claims,
legal bases, conditions, exceptions, and support relations instead of leaving them merged
in ordinary prose.

2.6.1

ADU-Inspired Triplets for Legal Evidence

The basic unit used here is inspired by the argumentative discourse unit (ADU), understood as a minimal segment that performs an argumentative function, such as stating
a claim, giving a legal basis, introducing a condition, or identifying an exception. In the
present thesis, this idea is operationalised in a deliberately lightweight form: retrieved
evidence is organised into triplets that connect a legal statement to its source-backed
support or limitation. A typical unit may therefore encode that a provision supports a
claim, that another provision qualifies it through a condition, or that an exception limits
its applicability.
This restricted use of argumentation theory is suﬀicient for the architecture. Toulmin’s
model is useful because it distinguishes conclusions from grounds, warrants, backing, qualifiers, and rebuttals (Toulmin, 1958); Walton’s schemes show that legal reasoning often
depends on recognisable forms such as authority, definition, analogy, and consequence
(Walton et al., 2008); and ASPIC+ formalises the defeasible nature of reasoning, where a
conclusion supported by a rule may still be defeated by an exception or stronger counterargument (Modgil and Prakken, 2014). The present framework does not attempt to reproduce these theories in full. It uses their shared insight: a legal answer should expose not
only the final conclusion, but also the source-backed relations that make the conclusion
valid, conditional, or defeasible.
In practical terms, ADU-inspired triplets provide an intermediate representation between raw retrieved text and final generation. They allow the system to distinguish,
for example, between a provision that states the main rule, a provision that defines a
key term, a provision that introduces an exception, and a provision that only provides

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

37

contextual background. This distinction is important because a generator that receives
all evidence as undifferentiated text may collapse legally distinct functions into a fluent
but unsupported answer. By contrast, structured evidence units make it easier to preserve legal roles during synthesis and to check whether each claim remains attached to an
identifiable source.

2.6.2

Progress, Limits, and the Algerian Constraint

Existing work supports the value of argument-aware representations, but also shows
why a bounded approach is necessary. Habernal et al., 2023 demonstrate that legal texts
can be annotated into fine-grained argumentative roles, but their large-scale European
Court of Human Rights corpus also illustrates the cost and expertise required for reliable
annotation. Ali et al., 2023 show that legal argument extraction benefits from structural constraints rather than unconstrained sequence labelling, while Elaraby and Litman,
2022 show that argument-role information can improve the faithfulness of legal summarisation. These results justify the use of argument-aware structure as an explainability and
generation-control mechanism.
The limitation is that such resources do not yet exist for Algerian law. Arabic legal
argument mining remains underdeveloped, and Abu Shairah et al., 2025 show that Arabic legal argument performance still lags behind better-resourced English settings. For
Algerian law, the constraint is sharper: there is no native, expert-annotated argumentmining corpus covering Arabic and French legal material. Arabic legal encoders such as
AraLegal-BERT make transfer more plausible (AL-Qurishi et al., 2022), but they do not
remove the need for jurisdiction-specific validation.
For this reason, the thesis adopts a modest and methodologically safer position.
Argument-aware evidence structuring is treated as a lightweight layer for organising
retrieved material before generation, not as a claim to solve full legal argument mining
for Algerian law. Its contribution is to improve traceability and synthesis: retrieved
provisions are not passed to the generator as flat context, but as structured evidence
units that clarify what each source contributes to the answer. This fits the broader
architecture of the thesis, where retrieval supplies candidate sources, the knowledge graph
expands legal dependencies, RLM-based orchestration controls the reasoning path, and
ADU-inspired triplets make the final evidence state more explicit before citation-faithful
generation.

Arabic Natural Language Processing for Legal
Text

The previous pillars establish the structural and retrieval foundations of the framework, but their effectiveness still depends on how Arabic legal text is represented at the
linguistic level. In the Algerian setting, this problem is sharpened by morphology, inconsistent orthographic conventions, and constant interaction between Arabic and French.
Arabic legal NLP is therefore not a secondary implementation detail. It is a cross-cutting
condition that shapes how well the corpus can be indexed, how reliably provisions can be
matched, and how much of a legal article survives into the model in the first place.

2.7.1

Tokenisation and Model Foundations

The first diﬀiculty appears before retrieval or generation begins: tokenisation. Modern
transformer models rely on subword segmentation methods such as Byte Pair Encoding,
WordPiece, and SentencePiece (Sennrich et al., 2016, Schuster and Nakajima, 2012, Kudo
and Richardson, 2018). In English, these schemes are usually eﬀicient enough that their
limitations remain relatively unobtrusive. In Arabic legal text, by contrast, tokenisation
directly affects what the model is able to read. Because Arabic is morphologically rich
and frequently agglutinative, a single orthographic word may encode several meaningful
components at once. This means that segmentation is not merely a preprocessing choice.
It determines how much of a provision fits into the model window and how much of its
internal legal structure remains visible to downstream components.
A first consequence is token budget inflation. Arabic legal text segmented with multilingual WordPiece tokenisers typically consumes substantially more tokens than the same
text segmented with Arabic-specific approaches. In practice, this means that a 512-token
encoder may contain only around 60–70% of a medium-length Arabic legal article, truncating conditions, exceptions, or penalty clauses before they ever reach the attention layers.
In legal NLP, this is not simply an eﬀiciency loss. It is a structural loss, because the
omitted part of the article may be precisely the part that determines its legal effect.
A second diﬀiculty concerns diacritics. Algerian legal Arabic is written almost entirely without vocalisation, while Arabic pretraining corpora often mix unvocalised prose
with material containing diacritics. This creates a tokenisation mismatch in which the
same lexical item may be segmented differently depending on whether the model encountered it in vocalised or unvocalised form during pretraining. The practical implication is
straightforward: consistent normalization becomes part of representation quality. In this
respect, the literature reports gains on the order of 3–7% when diacritics are stripped

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

39

uniformly before Arabic NLP processing, which makes diacritic normalization a justified
preprocessing step rather than a cosmetic adjustment (Al-Natsheh et al., 2022).
A third diﬀiculty is code-switching. Algerian legal and administrative text frequently
embeds French acronyms, French-style article identifiers, and Latin legal expressions inside
Arabic sentences. Strings such as SARL, Article L.311-1, or established legal Latinisms
should function as stable legal identifiers, not as arbitrary sequences of fragmented subwords. Yet no published tokeniser is specifically designed for Algerian Arabic–French
legal code-switching. In practice, this means that preprocessing heuristics are required to
detect and preserve legal identifiers before subword tokenisation is applied. Without such
handling, the model wastes token budget on breaking apart precisely those strings that
matter most for legal reference and retrieval.
These representational constraints shape the assessment of available models. The current landscape does not offer a single model that is simultaneously optimal for Arabic legal
language, bilingual Arabic–French alignment, French legal text, and generation. Instead,

2.7.2

Residual Gaps and Practical Constraints

Even with the strongest available combination of Arabic legal and multilingual models,
several limitations remain unresolved. The first is the Algerian domain gap. AraLegalBERT is the most relevant encoder available, but its 3.6% Algerian coverage means that
Algerian-specific terminology, citation practices, and institutional references remain systematically underrepresented in its learned space (AL-Qurishi et al., 2022). This does not
invalidate the model, but it places a clear limit on what can be expected from it without
further domain adaptation.
A second limitation is the bilingual cross-reference gap. Cross-lingual encoders can
often recognise that Arabic and French provisions are semantically close, but semantic
similarity is not the same as legal identity. Two language editions of the same law should
not merely be retrieved as similar texts; they should be treated as aligned manifestations
of the same legal object. Current multilingual encoders do not enforce that distinction on
their own. Edition alignment therefore requires explicit metadata and structural linking
rather than semantic similarity alone.
A third limitation is the temporal state gap. No current retrieval model represents
whether a provision is in force, amended, or repealed. A semantically relevant article may
therefore be retrieved regardless of whether it is legally valid for the date that matters.
Temporal filtering must be applied externally through structured metadata in the index.
This links the present section back to the data layer discussed earlier: without a structured
corpus carrying temporal information, even the strongest encoder remains temporally
blind.
A fourth limitation is the argument-relevance gap. Standard retrieval models are
trained to return passages that are topically similar to the query. Legal reasoning, however,
often depends on provisions that are not merely topically related but argumentatively
decisive, such as an exception, a limiting clause, or a premise that defeats the default rule.

Chapter 2. The Foundational Pillars

41

Cantador et al., 2021 show why this matters: retrieval guided by argumentative relevance
can improve legal question answering, but it requires annotated training data of the kind
that does not yet exist for Algerian law.
These limitations should be understood as practical constraints rather than objections.
They define the boundary conditions under which the architecture operates. Hybrid retrieval improves lexical and semantic access, but it does not resolve domain mismatch,
bilingual legal identity, temporal state, or argumentative relevance by itself. Those remaining gaps are precisely why the other pillars remain necessary together: structured
corpora for temporal filtering and versioning, knowledge graphs for explicit legal dependencies, and argument mining for inferential alignment.

2.8

Conclusion

Taken together, the foundations developed in this chapter define an architecture whose
logic is cumulative rather than optional. The data layer makes the legal corpus computationally usable, but structure alone does not retrieve relevant provisions or generate legally
grounded answers. IR and RAG provide the evidence-access layer, allowing the system
to locate relevant legal material and reduce dependence on parametric memory. Yet retrieval alone remains insuﬀicient when legal meaning depends on definitions, amendments,
cross-references, exceptions, and temporal validity.
The legal knowledge graph addresses this structural limitation by making legal dependencies explicit and queryable. It allows the system to move beyond isolated passages
toward traversable relations between provisions, legal concepts, citations, and amendments. However, a graph does not by itself determine how a complex legal question
should be decomposed, which evidentiary path should be followed, or when the available
material is insuﬀicient for a reliable answer. This is the role of RLM-based orchestration,
which coordinates retrieval, graph traversal, evidence inspection, path revision, and final
synthesis within a recursive reasoning loop.
Argument-aware evidence structuring then adds a further layer of discipline before
generation. Rather than functioning as a full standalone AM system, it organises retrieved and graph-expanded material into lightweight ADU-inspired units that separate
claims, legal bases, conditions, exceptions, and supporting citations. This improves the
inspectability of the final response and helps prevent the generator from collapsing legally
distinct elements into unsupported prose. Across all these foundations, Arabic legal NLP
remains a cross-cutting constraint, shaping tokenisation, representation, bilingual alignment, domain adaptation, retrieval quality, and generation reliability.
The architecture described in Chapter 3 is therefore not a design preference, but a
response to the specific requirements established throughout this chapter. Algerian legal

AI requires structured legal data, grounded evidence access, explicit legal relations, recursive reasoning control, and argument-aware organisation of evidence. Only by combining
these layers can the proposed framework move toward citation-faithful legal reasoning in
a low-resource, bilingual, and structurally complex legal environment.

When a source PDF was available, textual content was extracted using Apache Tika for
born-digital files and OCR for scanned documents. OCR-derived text was not accepted
as an unverified intermediate artifact: whenever OCR was required, the extracted text
was manually checked by one of the same two annotators involved in the benchmark
annotation process. A small subset of constitutional materials did not have a retained
PDF companion in the final dataset; for these texts, the oﬀicial textual version available
through the Algerian Constitutional Court website was used directly as the source text.
This distinction is important methodologically: the corpus is not a uniform “PDF-only”
dataset, but a provenance-preserving legal-text corpus whose source material may be
either an oﬀicial publication PDF or an oﬀicial institutional text page.
The resulting text was transformed into LegalDocML / Akoma Ntoso through a semiautomated conversion pipeline. The pipeline first performs deterministic preprocessing
and parsing to identify the preamble, legal hierarchy, article inventory, article numbers,
article statuses, line ranges, and parent structural paths. This deterministic parse establishes the article-level inventory used during conversion. The language model is then used
only in a constrained role: it converts bounded preamble and article chunks into XML
fragments while the parser-provided identifiers, expected articles, and hierarchy remain
externally controlled. In other words, the model does not decide which legal articles exist
in the corpus; it only assists in marking up already identified legal units.

Quality control was performed at several levels. At the automated level, generated
XML fragments were checked for well-formedness, article structure, paragraph and list
structure, semantic-tag constraints, reference attributes, and content-integrity conditions
such as missing or duplicate expected article identifiers. Invalid chunks could be retried
with explicit validation feedback, and prompts, raw responses, retries, and validation
results were persisted at chunk granularity. At the human-audit level, the final Akoma
Ntoso files were reviewed structurally by the author section by section, chapter by chapter,
and book by book. This audit focused on preserving the legal hierarchy, verifying article placement, detecting conversion failures, and ensuring that the XML representation
remained faithful to the underlying legal text. The resulting AKN layer is therefore best
understood as a curated, inspectable legal corpus rather than as a simple OCR-to-XML
export.
This is the practical realization of the corpus-structuring requirement established in
Chapter 2: the law must first be turned into structured legal objects before retrieval,
graph construction, citation verification, or legal reasoning can operate reliably (OASIS
LegalDocML TC, 2018; Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024; Küçük and Can, 2025).

A central practical issue, revealed during corpus construction, was canonical identity. The final system does not treat filenames as legally authoritative identifiers. Instead,
canonical identity is derived from the FRBR metadata encoded in the Akoma Ntoso XML
and then normalized through a registry alias layer. This matters because several files use
surface names that differ from the legally meaningful identifier. For example, the Civil
Code is stored under the filename stem 75-8_1975-09-26, while the canonical legal identifier is 75-58_1975-09-26. Constitutional texts likewise use descriptive filename stems
such as constitution_2020-12-30, while the registry must map them to their corresponding canonical legal identity. The alias layer therefore prevents the system from confusing
file storage conventions with legal identity, and it turns the curated Akoma Ntoso layer
into the authoritative registry from which runtime citation validity is later checked.
The same principle applies to legacy names, alternative Arabic references, and
benchmark-facing document identifiers. In the final architecture, a cited provision must
resolve through the article registry before it can be accepted as a valid legal citation.
This registry is built from the Akoma Ntoso layer rather than from filenames alone. It

therefore provides a hard corpus boundary for the rest of the system: retrieval may

can be treated as legally valid unless it resolves to a registered document and article.

in Chapter 1.
The corpus should not be read as an oﬀicial legal consolidation or as a claim of exhaustive semantic annotation. Its contribution is methodological and computational: it
provides a structured, manually audited, registry-backed legal corpus over which retrieval,
graph construction, citation verification, and benchmark evaluation can be performed reproducibly.
Once this corpus was constructed in Akoma Ntoso form, it could be transformed into a
legal knowledge graph supporting retrieval, temporal versioning, cross-reference traversal,
and citation verification.

3.2.2

The Legal Knowledge Graph

The RDF layer is a derived representation of the Akoma Ntoso corpus rather than
a second independently curated source. It consists of document-level Turtle exports together with one consolidated graph file, algerian_legal_kg.ttl, giving the graph layer 46
RDF/Turtle files in total. The live corpus and the RDF export are synchronized at
the structured-text level: each Akoma Ntoso legal text has a corresponding text companion and document-level RDF representation, while source-PDF availability depends on
whether an oﬀicial PDF was retained for that instrument. The graph therefore no longer
represents only a partial subset of the XML corpus; it is the structured graph projection
of the same curated legal-text base.
The purpose of the graph is not merely to duplicate the XML structure in another
format. Its role is to formalize legal relations in a queryable form. Akoma Ntoso provides
structured legal documents; RDF makes their entities, references, amendment relations,
article ancestry, and legal concepts available as graph triples. This is the methodological
step that turns structured legal documents into a navigable legal substrate (Hoekstra et al.,
2007; Küçük and Can, 2025; Hamouda Sidhoum et al., 2024; d’Amato et al., 2025).
The graph plays three distinct roles in the architecture. First, it acts as a canonical
corpus boundary. Every graph node inherits its legal identity from an Akoma Ntoso
document and can therefore be aligned with the same FRBR-based registry used for
runtime citation verification. This directly constrains one of the central failure modes
identified in Chapter 1: the system cannot cite a provision that does not exist in the legal
registry without that error becoming detectable. In the final implementation, the hard
boundary is enforced by the article registry, while the RDF graph provides a semantically

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

48

richer representation of the same corpus boundary.
Second, the graph acts as the substrate for amendment-aware retrieval and temporal versioning. Legal texts are not static objects. A provision may be introduced,
amended, repealed, or partially superseded while retaining a stable legal identity. In a flat
retrieval pipeline, such changes remain diﬀicult to track because the retriever sees only
text segments. In the graph, amendment relations and article versions can be represented
as explicit edges. This makes it possible to traverse from a provision to its historical or
current legal states and to recover the version that is legally relevant for a given date. The
importance of this role is methodological as much as technical: Chapters 1 and 2 established that temporality is one of the defining challenges of legal AI. A retrieved article is
not necessarily the legally valid article for the queried time slice. By representing amendment chains explicitly, the graph enables date-aware resolution rather than static-text
retrieval alone (Colombo et al., 2025; Küçük and Can, 2025).
Third, the graph encodes cross-reference and intertextual relations as resolvable
RDF triples rather than as textual mentions embedded in article prose. This directly
addresses the structural blindness of ordinary passage retrieval. In a flat text pipeline, a
phrase such as “as defined in Article 52” remains only a textual fragment. In the graph,
it can become a typed and traversable relation linking one article resource to another.
This is the point at which legal interdependence becomes computationally available. It
also clarifies the exact methodological scope of the graph: the graph makes structural
dependencies explicit and queryable, but it does not by itself determine how a chain of
provisions supports a legal conclusion. That remaining task is handled by the RLM-based
reasoning controller and the argument-aware evidence-structuring layer introduced later
in the chapter.
The amendment-chain logic can be illustrated by the Family Code’s Article 54 and
its 2005 reform. In the graph, the pre-amendment state of Article 54, the 2005 amending
instrument, and the post-amendment state of the same article are not stored as loose
textual artifacts. They are linked through an explicit version chain. This means that a
system can deterministically traverse from an article identifier to its historical and current
legal states rather than guessing which version to apply from semantic similarity alone.

3.3

The AlgerianLegalBench Benchmark

AlgerianLegalBench is the evaluation layer of the thesis. It was constructed to test
whether a system can answer Algerian legal questions under the constraints established
in Chapters 1 and 2: retrieval must recover the correct legal instruments, citations must
resolve to the correct articles, generated answers must remain supported by cited sources,
and the system must abstain when the corpus does not justify an answer. The benchmark
therefore evaluates legal answering as a structured task rather than as ordinary openended question answering.
Each item pairs a natural-language legal question with evaluation-facing signals: expected documents, expected articles, answerability metadata, a reference answer, a fourstep reasoning chain, and annotation metadata. This design allows the same benchmark to
support retrieval evaluation, article-level citation evaluation, answer-quality assessment,
abstention analysis, and reasoning-chain comparison. The section first defines the benchmark design goals, then reports its composition and statistics, and finally describes the
gold annotation structure used during evaluation.
The annotation protocol is one of the benchmark’s main academic contributions.
Each question was not treated as a simple question–answer pair, but as a structured legal
evaluation unit. For every item, the author first wrote the question, the ground-truth
answer, and a draft reasoning chain. A second annotator then independently completed or
reviewed the reasoning chain using only the question and the relevant legal texts. When
disagreement exceeded the accepted threshold, a senior legal expert resolved the item.
This cross-annotation design is important because it reduces the risk that the benchmark
simply reproduces the assumptions of its original author, and it makes the legal support
structure of each item explicit.

Design Goals

The first design goal was retrieval-grounded legal evaluation. The benchmark
is not a general legal question-answering dataset in which the answer alone is suﬀicient. Each question contains gold retrieval targets through expected_documents and
expected_articles. This allows the evaluation to measure whether a system retrieves the
correct legal instruments and provisions before generating an answer. The design follows
the central methodological claim of this thesis: legal AI cannot be evaluated only by
surface answer quality, because an answer may be fluent while relying on the wrong code,
the wrong article, or no legal support at all.
The second design goal was article-level citation faithfulness. Document-level
relevance is too weak for legal reasoning. A system that retrieves the Family Code but
cites the wrong article should not be treated as fully correct, because legal justification
depends on precise support. For that reason, the benchmark records expected article
references for each answerable item, including document identifiers, article references,
optional disambiguated article references, Arabic law names, and dataset-presence flags.
This makes it possible to evaluate citation precision, citation recall, hallucinated citations,
and answer–citation support at the article level rather than only at the document level.
The third design goal was coverage of heterogeneous legal query types. The
benchmark reflects the taxonomy introduced in Chapter 1: some legal questions are direct article lookups, others require rule application, conceptual definition, multi-hop reasoning, long-context synthesis, temporal awareness, layman-language interpretation, or
abstention. This heterogeneity is important because a system that performs well on exact retrieval may still fail on compositional legal reasoning, and a system that answers
ordinary questions fluently may still hallucinate when faced with an unanswerable one.
The fourth design goal was multilingual realism. Algerian legal practice is not
fully monolingual. The benchmark therefore includes both Modern Standard Arabic and
French. Arabic remains dominant, reflecting the practical composition of the corpus and
the post-independence statutory environment, while French is retained as a smaller but
legally relevant slice. This design avoids artificial symmetry while still testing whether
the system can operate under the bilingual conditions of Algerian legal information.
The fifth design goal was robustness under diﬀicult retrieval and reasoning
conditions. The benchmark includes multi-hop, long-context, temporal, and intentionally unanswerable questions. These are not peripheral additions. They operationalize the
failure modes diagnosed earlier in the thesis: retrieval insuﬀiciency, structural blindness,
temporal fragility, jurisdictional contamination, and unsafe answer generation when the
corpus does not support a response. In particular, the unanswerable slice tests whether a
system can abstain instead of importing unsupported or foreign legal concepts.

The sixth design goal was regenerability from a canonical benchmark surface.
AlgerianLegalBench v3.0 is treated as the authoritative benchmark-first dataset. Downstream artifacts such as valid-only retrieval sets, excluded sets, alignment reports, and
retriever outputs should be regenerated from it whenever the gold annotations or legal
registry are updated. This avoids treating derived evaluation files as independent sources
of truth and keeps the benchmark aligned with the live corpus registry.

3.4

System Architecture

The proposed system operationalizes the thesis methodology as a typed-handler architecture over a structured Algerian legal environment. Rather than applying a uniform
retrieve-then-generate sequence to every query, the system routes each question to a handler specialized for its query type. Each handler can invoke shared legal primitives for
retrieval, graph traversal, article-registry checks, sub-model verification, argument-aware
evidence structuring, and faithfulness control. This makes the architecture adaptive without making it unconstrained: the language models plan, decompose, verify, and synthesize, while citation existence, corpus boundaries, graph relations, and faithfulness checks
remain externally enforced.
Figure 3.2 summarises the full execution flow.
At runtime, the system proceeds through four logical stages. First, the input layer
normalizes the query, preserves legal identifiers, and classifies the question into one of the
benchmark query types. Second, the dispatcher performs document routing and, when
needed, decomposition into smaller legal sub-questions. Third, the selected typed handler
invokes the legal environment: hybrid retrieval, graph traversal, article lookup, bounded
sub-model workers, and argument-aware evidence preparation. Fourth, the synthesized
answer is checked by three faithfulness gates: citation existence, jurisdictional faithfulness,
and claim-level support. If claim-level support fails, the system performs one bounded
corrective regeneration before either returning a verified answer or abstaining.
The architecture is therefore organized around two design principles. The first is that
the system is deterministic outside, generative inside. Operations that define legal boundaries are implemented deterministically: article-registry checks, canonical document resolution, graph traversal, citation existence, and corpus-boundary verification. Language
models are reserved for tasks requiring semantic flexibility, such as query interpretation,
decomposition, relevance judgement, evidence synthesis, and explanation. The second
principle is bounded recursion. Recursive refinement is allowed only through typed handlers, capped depth, coverage thresholds, and a single corrective retry. This prevents the
RLM layer from becoming open-ended while preserving its methodological role: controlling evidence construction before final generation.

3.4.1

Query Intake and Normalization

The first stage of the architecture is input normalization. This stage exists because,
as argued in §2.5.1, Arabic legal text is highly sensitive to orthographic inconsistency,
morphological variation, and code-switching effects that can corrupt both sparse and
dense retrieval if left untreated. Normalization is therefore not a cosmetic preprocessing
step; it is retrieval-critical and citation-critical.
For Arabic queries, normalization begins with Unicode canonicalization in NFC form.
The system then unifies the hamza variants U+0623, U+0625, and U+0622 to bare
U+0627, normalizes U+0649 to U+064A, handles tā’ marbūt a consistently, strips diacritics including shadda, short-vowel marks, and tatweel, converts Arabic-Indic digits to
ASCII digits, and collapses extraneous whitespace. These operations follow directly from
the linguistic diagnosis of Chapter 2: consistent normalization reduces the mismatch between legal surface forms and the tokenization regime used by the retrievers and language
models. In particular, the thesis already established that diacritic stripping is not merely
orthographic simplification but a justified preprocessing operation associated with measurable gains in Arabic NLP (Al-Natsheh et al., 2022; Antoun et al., 2020; Inoue et al.,
2021).
For French queries, normalization also begins with NFC canonicalization, followed
by casefolding. Accent stripping is handled more cautiously: the system preserves both
accent-sensitive and accent-stripped forms for retrieval, so that lexical matching is not
weakened when the query is entered in a degraded or keyboard-constrained form. This is
especially relevant in mixed Arabic–French legal practice, where professional users may
switch language or script conventions even when referring to the same legal object.
A further complication is code-switching. Chapter 2 already noted that Algerian legal
queries frequently embed French acronyms, mixed-language entity names, and legal identifiers such as article numbers and law references inside Arabic prose. The input layer
therefore performs a lightweight identifier-preservation pass before subword tokenization.
Legal identifiers (SARL, article-number patterns, mixed French legal tokens) are detected
and preserved as atomic retrieval-relevant units rather than being fragmented into arbitrary subword pieces. This improves both exact matching and cross-lingual stability,
while reducing context waste for downstream model calls.

Once normalized, the query is classified into one of the eight benchmark query types

3.4.2

Intent Classification and Decomposition

Intent classification allows the dispatcher to select the appropriate handler before
retrieval begins. The classifier assigns each query to one of the eight benchmark query
types: exact article, rule application, conceptual-definitional, temporal-factual, multi-hop,
long-context, layman-language, or unanswerable. It also emits three auxiliary signals:
the working language, a temporal-intent flag, and an answerability hint. These signals
determine whether the system should perform ordinary retrieval, activate amendmentaware graph traversal, decompose the question, or apply stricter abstention safeguards.
Two classifier families were considered. A deterministic regex baseline was used as a
minimal reference because it can capture explicit markers such as article-number patterns
or temporal expressions, but it cannot reliably distinguish semantically close classes such
as exact-article and rule-application questions. The deployed configuration therefore uses
a few-shot classifier based on google/gemma-4-31B. Its role is not to answer the legal
question, but to assign a routing type that determines the downstream reasoning path.
The quantitative comparison between the regex and few-shot classifiers is reported in
Chapter 4.
For multi-hop and long-context questions, the classification stage may also trigger
decomposition. A bounded sub-model call, delegated to the Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking
decomposer described in §3.4.4, rewrites the original query into smaller legal sub-questions.
Each sub-question is tagged with candidate target codes and any temporal requirements
inferred from the query. This step is used only when the query type requires it: its
purpose is to separate complex legal reasoning into individually retrievable and verifiable
units before recombination.
The classified and, when necessary, decomposed query is then passed to the appropriate typed handler, which operates over the legal environment described next.

3.4.3

The Legal Environment as Callable Primitives

The core novelty of the architecture is concentrated in how the typed handler interacts with the legal environment. Rather than issuing each retrieval and verification step
automatically through a fixed pipeline, the handler treats the environment as a set of
callable primitives that it can compose adaptively, in the spirit of the recursive-languagemodel paradigm of ?. The present implementation realizes this idea in-process rather
than through a Python Application Programming Interface (API) sandboxed subprocess:

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

59

the primitives are exposed as methods on a shared environment object, and the dispatcher
selects among them according to the handler’s strategy. This trade-off is deliberate. A
free-form Python interpreter would expose the model to syntactic failure modes and unbounded action spaces; the in-process primitive interface keeps the architecture recursive
in the methodological sense (planning, sub-call, evidence, re-planning) while remaining
controllable in the legal sense (every action is auditable, every output is registry-checked).
The first family of primitives is the retrieval family. The locked deployable configuration uses three retrieval channels fused by Reciprocal Rank Fusion (Reciprocal Rank
Fusion (RRF), defined in Equation ?? earlier in the chapter): BM25 with a legal-identifier
tokenizer, dense retrieval with intfloat/multilingual-e5-small over a FAISS index, and Hypothetical Document Embeddings (HyDE) in which a Qwen3 draft of a hypothetical
answer is concatenated with the query before dense encoding. Their coexistence is not
redundant. BM25 is retained for its strength on exact identifiers, law numbers, and article references; dense retrieval handles paraphrase and Arabic morphological variation;
HyDE closes the gap between underspecified user queries and the formal phrasing of the
underlying articles. HyDE is applied selectively: it is disabled for temporal-factual and
conceptual-definitional queries because those handlers anchor on the knowledge graph,
and pilot measurements showed that HyDE-rewriting drifts the dense channel away from
the in-force article version. RRF fuses the channels using the parameterization established
earlier (Cormack et al., 2009; Robertson and Zaragoza, 2009; Karpukhin et al., 2020; Gao
et al., 2023b). Two additional channels — Sparse Lexical and Expansion model (SPLADE)
sparse expansion and ColBERT late-interaction retrieval — exist in the codebase behind
feature flags and were evaluated during baseline construction, but they are not part of the
deployable configuration; they are revisited as future-work directions in general conclusion
5.5 (Formal et al., 2021a; Khattab and Zaharia, 2020).
The second family is the knowledge-graph family. It exposes parameterized
SPARQL queries, a surface-form-to-canonical-ID entity resolver, amendment-chain
traversal over dated transitive edges, and an article-at-date lookup that retrieves the
version of a provision in force at a query-relevant date. These primitives are what allow
the dispatcher to move from merely relevant text toward legally valid and temporally
appropriate text. They operationalize the claim made in Chapter 2 that retrieval
alone cannot recover amendment history or deterministic legal dependency without a
structured graph substrate (Edge et al., 2025; Colombo et al., 2025).
The third family is the article-operations family: full article-text retrieval by canonical identifier, enumeration of the registry-defined corpus boundary, and a deterministic
article-existence check. The handler is never forced to infer corpus boundaries from parametric memory; it queries them explicitly. In a legal system where hallucinated citations
are common, this hard registry boundary is one of the most important deterministic
safeguards in the architecture.

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

60

The fourth family is the recursive-call family. Its principal primitive is a sub-model
invocation that spawns a bounded worker with its own role-specific prompt and a constrained context window. The recursive primitive is what allows the architecture to use
different workers for decomposition, verification, summarization, and ADU extraction
without collapsing all reasoning into a single monolithic generator.
The fifth family is the verification family. It includes a thin wrapper over the articleexistence check and a jurisdiction-infection detector built from the canary list derived
directly from the 40 infection traps in AlgerianLegalBench. The list includes French
civil-law concepts such as ISF, garde à vue, encadrement des loyers, EURL, SAS, and
rupture conventionnelle; US common-law concepts such as at-will employment, punitive
damages, fair use, stare decisis, and plea bargaining; Egyptian inheritance concepts such
as waṣiyya wājiba; Tunisian family-law concepts such as Chapter 18 of the Personal Status
Code; Gulf labour concepts such as the kafī l sponsorship regime; and several concepts
whose absence from Algerian law is itself the benchmark trap, including constitutional
auto-saisine, municipal taxing power, and the death penalty for corruption. Jurisdictional
faithfulness is therefore not handled only at the end of generation; it is already part of
the environment the handler can query before committing to an answer.
The sixth and final primitive is argumentative discourse unit extraction (ADU),
the bridge to the argument-mining layer described in §3.4.6. It does not attempt to build
a full gold-standard legal argument graph. Instead, it extracts structured Toulmin-style
units from cited evidence so that the answer can be expressed through the same reasoning
templates used in the benchmark and verified at the claim level rather than as one opaque
block of generated prose.
Recursion is constrained by a per-handler budget. The dispatcher caps gap-probe recursion at depth three; a per-handler coverage floor prevents the gap-probe from firing on
candidate pools that are already saturated; and faithfulness-driven corrective regeneration
is allowed exactly once per query. These bounds are part of the locked methodological configuration. Their empirical effect on recursion frequency, latency, and final performance
is reported in Chapter 4.
A typical trajectory can be illustrated with an infection-trap example such as rent
control. In that case, the unanswerable handler first invokes the jurisdiction-infection
detector on encadrement des loyers. When the canary fires, the handler queries the
registry-defined corpus boundary and the housing/civil retrieval channels to verify whether
any Algerian instrument contains the imported doctrine. When the registry and retrieved
evidence confirm absence rather than support, the handler emits a structured abstention
rather than a guessed answer. The refusal is not generated; it is constructed from corpusboundary verification and explicit absence checking.
The recursive primitive is what makes the architecture distinctive. Its operation
through the root controller and sub-model workers is described next.

The Root Controller and Sub-LM Workers

The root controller is a single planning model (gpt-oss-120b) governed by a system
prompt whose role is not merely to answer legal questions, but to enforce the architecture’s
methodological commitments. Three instructions are central. First, the controller is forbidden from emitting any citation that cannot be verified through the article-existence
primitive. Second, it must invoke the jurisdiction-infection detector early whenever the
answerability hint or query phrasing suggests a possible foreign-law or out-of-corpus concept. Third, it must produce a four-step reasoning chain aligned with the benchmark
templates, so that its output is structurally comparable to the benchmark annotation
rather than a free-form paragraph detached from the evaluation logic.
The reasoning templates used by the root controller are exactly the four templates
defined in AlgerianLegalBench. Template A is the standard four-step chain used for exactarticle, rule-application, conceptual-definitional, and layman-language questions: legal issue, applicable rule, application/subsumption, conclusion. Template B is the temporal
template: version identification, amendment timeline, correct version applied, modelfailure description. Template C is the multi-hop template: name the chain, hop 1, hop 2,
synthesis plus failure point. Template D is the unanswerable template: failure mode,
demonstration of absence, incorrect infected answer, correct abstaining response. The
controller selects among these templates based on the query-type tag produced upstream.
The benchmark therefore does not only evaluate the system downstream; it shapes the
controller’s output format upstream.
The root model is also given a small set of few-shot trajectories. The design deliberately uses only three: one retrieve-and-cite trajectory for direct legal lookup, one chainand-synthesize trajectory for compositional reasoning, and one abstention trajectory for
infection or genuine-absence cases. This is the smallest few-shot set that still exposes the
controller to all three behavioural modes required by the benchmark, while preserving
enough root-context budget for real evidence and tool feedback.
Below the root controller the system uses three main sub-model prompt families. The
verifier worker receives a sub-question and one candidate article and returns a structured
relevance judgement together with a supporting span. The summarizer worker receives
a sub-question and a list of candidate articles and returns a faithful short synthesis with
explicit article references. The decomposer worker receives a complex query and returns
smaller atomic sub-questions with code and temporal tags. All three families are served by
Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking, chosen for its structured-output stability under JSON parsing
constraints. All sub-model outputs are forced through strict JSON parsing. On parse
failure the system allows one self-correction round; if that fails too, it logs the failure and
returns a degraded but well-formed default object so the pipeline remains structurally

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

62

safe rather than silently breaking.
The architecture therefore uses multiple language models, but not in an unconstrained
or homogeneous way. The root controller plans and delegates. Workers execute bounded
roles. Deterministic checks enforce legality of citations and boundaries of the corpus. This
division of labour is what allows the architecture to remain generative without becoming
ungoverned.
Every output of the root controller passes through three independent faithfulness gates
before it can be emitted.

3.4.5

The Triple Faithfulness Gate

Every generated answer must pass three independent faithfulness gates before it can
be emitted. These gates are not post-hoc presentation filters; they are runtime constraints
that determine whether the answer is accepted, revised, or replaced by a safe abstention.
The first gate is citation existence. It is fully deterministic. Every emitted citation
in the form (doc_id, article_ref ) must resolve in the article registry before it can be
accepted. This gate addresses fabricated or malformed legal citations by enforcing the
corpus boundary directly: if a cited article does not exist in the live registry, the answer
cannot pass.
The second gate is jurisdictional faithfulness. It combines the jurisdiction-infection
detector described in §3.4.3 with a lightweight classifier over the final answer text. If
foreign-law or out-of-corpus signals are detected, the answer must either abstain correctly
or demonstrate support from Algerian legal sources. This gate prevents the system from
importing unsupported concepts from other jurisdictions when Algerian law does not
justify them.
The third gate is answer–citation support verification. The system segments
the answer into claim units and scores each claim against the cited article texts using
a multilingual Natural Language Inference (NLI) model, MoritzLaurer/mDeBERTa-v3base-mnli-xnli. A claim is treated as supported when at least one cited article entails
it above the calibrated threshold θ = 0.55. This gate addresses the deeper attribution
problem: a citation must not merely exist; it must support the claim attached to it.
When the third gate fails, the system performs a single corrective regeneration
step. The unsupported claim units are passed back to the summarizer with explicit
feedback requiring the revised answer to use only the cited article texts. The regenerated
answer is then evaluated by the same gates. If it still fails, the dispatcher emits a safe
abstention stating that the answer could not be verified from the corpus. The one-retry
limit is part of the locked configuration and prevents corrective generation from becoming
open-ended.

3.4.6

Argument-Aware Evidence Structuring and the Reasoning
Chain

Argument-aware evidence structuring is integrated through a lightweight ADUinspired extraction step applied to the top cited articles selected by each handler. The
component does not attempt to construct a full legal argument graph or to solve Algerian
legal argument mining as an independent task. Its function is narrower: it converts cited
evidence into structured units that distinguish claims, legal grounds, warrants, rebuttals,
and backing before final synthesis.
The extracted units serve two roles. First, they support explanatory display. They
are reorganized into the four-step reasoning chains used by AlgerianLegalBench, allowing
the system to present not only an answer but also the path from legal issue, to applicable
rule, to application, to conclusion. For temporal, multi-hop, and unanswerable questions,
the same logic supports version timelines, chained legal hops, and structured abstentions.
Second, the units support verification. Claim-level units extracted from the answer
are passed to the third faithfulness gate, where they are checked against cited legal grounds
through multilingual NLI. This gives the argument-aware layer a dual methodological role:
it improves the inspectability of the answer and provides the units on which claim-level
support verification operates. The resulting faithfulness metrics are defined in §3.5.1 and
reported in Chapter 4.
A limitation remains. The system does not rely on a native Algerian-Arabic gold
benchmark for legal argument mining. The extraction therefore depends on transferred
resources and prompt-based generalization rather than on jurisdiction-specific annotated
argument graphs. The methodology compensates by validating the extracted units indirectly through the article registry, benchmark reasoning templates, and faithfulness gates,
but it does not claim to solve Algerian legal argument mining as a standalone supervised
task.

3.5

Evaluation Methodology

The evaluation protocol follows the central claim of the thesis: legal competence
cannot be reduced to a single answer-quality score. A system may retrieve the correct
document but cite the wrong article, cite a real article but infer an unsupported rule,
answer plausible questions while importing foreign-law concepts, or abstain safely at the
cost of excessive refusal. The evaluation therefore separates the system’s behaviour into
distinct metric families: retrieval, citation, generation, faithfulness, abstention, and RLMspecific runtime telemetry.
This section defines those metrics, explains how they are reported across benchmark
strata, specifies the statistical comparison procedure, and describes the baseline and ablation design used in Chapter 4. The purpose is not only to measure whether the proposed
system improves over baselines, but to identify which part of the legal reasoning pipeline
improves: evidence access, citation control, jurisdictional discipline, claim support, abstention behaviour, or recursive orchestration cost.

3.5.1

Metrics

The metric design follows the logic of the benchmark itself: each family corresponds to
a distinct aspect of legal-system behaviour. Retrieval metrics measure whether relevant
legal evidence is found at all. Citation metrics measure whether the system names the correct legal support. Generation metrics measure textual overlap and answer-form quality.
Faithfulness metrics measure whether the answer is actually supported by its citations and
whether it respects jurisdictional boundaries. Abstention metrics measure whether the
system rejects infected or absent-answer questions correctly. Runtime telemetry measures
the cost of recursive orchestration and is necessary to interpret the cost–quality trade-off
honestly.

3.5.3

Statistical Methodology

For every metric reported in Chapter 4, the study computes 95% bootstrap con-

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

72

fidence intervals over 1000 resamples of the benchmark. This choice is motivated
by the benchmark size and by the mixture of binary, ranking, and overlap-based metrics
used in the study. Bootstrap intervals are more appropriate here than asymptotic approximations because the evaluation includes several non-Gaussian distributions, especially on
the unanswerable and temporal subsets.
For paired comparisons between the proposed system and each baseline, the evaluation
reports paired bootstrap p-values. The significance threshold is set at
α = 0.05,
with Bonferroni correction applied across the metric × system comparison grid. Significance is not reported alone: each comparison is accompanied by an effect size, because
with a benchmark of 244 questions statistical significance is not equivalent to practical
significance. A small gain on a large subset may be significant but unimportant, whereas
a larger gain on a strategically critical subset may matter more even if its confidence
interval is wider.
The development split is used for ablation tuning and threshold calibration, including
the per-citation NLI support threshold θ = 0.55, the per-handler recursion_coverage_min
value, and the choice of deployable ablation configuration. The test split is reserved
exclusively for headline comparisons against the baseline systems. This split discipline is
consistent with the κ < 0.60 separation already argued in §3.3.3, where the development
split was explicitly designed to absorb the more interpretively ambiguous items.

3.5.4

Baselines and Ablations

The comparison design contains three baseline tiers and one family of internal ablations. Each tier addresses a different possible explanation for the proposed system’s
performance: direct language-model capability, retrieval-only strength on the same corpus, or the contribution of individual architectural components.
Tier 1 — direct-LLM baselines

The first tier evaluates direct prompting of open and proprietary language models on
AlgerianLegalBench without access to the structured retrieval and verification pipeline.
This tier tests whether the task can be solved by parametric model knowledge and general
legal reasoning alone. All direct-LLM baselines are evaluated with the same benchmark
file, prompt family, and evaluator used for the proposed system. Their quantitative results
are reported in Chapter 4.

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

73

Tier 2 — deterministic and RAG-only baselines on the same corpus

The second tier evaluates non-recursive baselines over the same Algerian legal corpus, knowledge graph, indices, and benchmark. The deterministic retrieval baselines
include BM25, dense retrieval, hybrid RRF fusion, hybrid retrieval with reranking, a
pure SPARQL knowledge-graph baseline, and a graph-plus-retrieval fusion baseline. The
RAG-only baselines pair retrieved context with a generator but do not use typed handlers,
bounded recursion, argument-aware evidence structuring, or the full faithfulness-gating
loop. This tier isolates the effect of the proposed architecture from the effect of simply
giving the model access to the corpus.
Tier 3 — internal phase ablations

The third tier consists of controlled cuts of the proposed architecture. These ablations
test the marginal contribution of classifier-typed routing, gold-typed routing, pervasive
ADU extraction, gap-driven recursion, corrective retry, knowledge-graph channels, denseencoder choice, selective HyDE, and per-handler recursion thresholds. Additional safety
ablations remove the jurisdiction gate or the NLI support gate to test how much of the
final faithfulness behaviour depends on each runtime constraint.
Together, the three tiers make the comparison conservative. Tier 1 asks whether general LLMs can solve the benchmark directly. Tier 2 asks whether retrieval and simple
RAG over the same corpus are suﬀicient. Tier 3 asks which parts of the proposed architecture are actually responsible for any observed gains. The numerical results of all
baselines and ablations are presented in Chapter 4.

3.6

Threats to Validity

Every methodology has limits, and naming them protects the conclusions of Chapter 4
from being read more strongly than the evidence supports. The purpose of the present
section is therefore not defensive disclaimer, but methodological calibration. The preceding sections described a structured Akoma Ntoso corpus, a derived legal knowledge graph,
a domain-specific benchmark, and a typed-handler recursive-language-model architecture
with explicit faithfulness controls. Those elements make the system substantially stronger
than flat retrieval or direct generation alone, but they do not eliminate uncertainty. Some

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

75

threats arise from the corpus itself, some from the benchmark and its annotation protocol, and some from the fact that legal correctness is only partially captured by automated
metrics. These limits are grouped below into internal, external, and construct validity.

3.6.1

Internal Validity

The first internal threat concerns annotation ambiguity in the hardest reasoning slices. The benchmark achieves strong overall agreement, with Cohen’s κ = 0.829,
and very high agreement on easy, medium, and exact-article questions (κ = 0.979, 0.904,
and 0.967 respectively). However, agreement drops to κ = 0.582 on multi-hop questions
and κ = 0.574 on long-context questions. These values fall below the conventional threshold for substantial agreement and create a real possibility that some held-out “correct”
answers are themselves contestable or partially mislabelled. The benchmark design mitigates this through the κ < 0.60 split policy: items below that threshold are placed in
development rather than test, yielding a test set of 182 questions and a development set
of 62. This mitigation is important, but it does not eliminate the threat. First, κ is
measured over the annotation process, not over final model outputs. Second, even in
the test split, some structurally diﬀicult legal questions remain close to the threshold of
interpretive disagreement. This is therefore a genuine limit on the precision with which
system differences can be interpreted on the hardest compositional slices. The point is
not that the benchmark is unreliable; it is that legal multi-hop reasoning is intrinsically
less annotation-stable than direct statutory lookup, and the evaluation should be read
with that asymmetry in mind.
The second internal threat is a mild form of evaluation contamination in the jurisdiction gate. The infection-canary list used by the architecture’s jurisdiction-infection
detector was compiled from the same forty unanswerable questions that later serve as
the evaluation set for that detector. Methodologically, this means one component of
the runtime guard has been informed by the exact surface forms it is later tested on.
The contamination is limited rather than fatal: the gate operates only as a signal, the
deterministic registry boundary and retrieval-based absence verification must still pass
before an abstention is emitted, and a fired canary on an answerable question still requires the rest of the architecture to confirm the foreign concept. Even so, the risk is real.
Strong performance on the unanswerable subset could partly reflect direct lexical familiarity with benchmark canaries rather than more general jurisdictional reasoning. The
methodological commitment in §3.5.4 therefore includes a no-jurisdiction-gate ablation:
unanswerable-slice results are reported both with the canary signals active and with those
signals removed, so the size of the canary effect is exposed rather than concealed.
Taken together, these two internal threats do not invalidate the evaluation, but they

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

76

narrow the strength of the claims that can be drawn from it. The hardest reasoning slices
remain annotation-sensitive; and the unanswerable subset benefits to some extent from
a runtime component informed by that same subset. The conclusions of Chapter 4 are
stated with those bounds in place.

3.6.2

External Validity

The first external-validity threat is the limited size of the benchmark. AlgerianLegalBench contains 244 questions across 23 legal categories, which is suﬀicient for the
thesis’s controlled comparisons but still small by the standards of broad industrial evaluation. The limitation is most acute in the tail of the category distribution: some legal
categories contain too few questions to support reliable category-specific conclusions. For
this reason, Chapter 4 treats small categories cautiously, reporting them in aggregate or
using them illustratively rather than as independent evidence of system-level performance.
The second external-validity threat is that the corpus is a snapshot rather than a
live legal stream. The Akoma Ntoso corpus and the derived knowledge graph represent
the legal state captured at the time of corpus freeze. Amendments enacted after that
freeze are not represented, and the temporal index inherits the same cutoff. The system’s
temporal correctness is therefore evaluated only relative to the corpus snapshot, not relative to legislation enacted afterward. The architecture is amendment-aware within its
corpus boundary, but it is not chronologically open-ended. This does not weaken the
methodological contribution of the thesis, which is about structured legal reasoning over
a defined corpus, but it does limit any claim that the system can answer current-law
questions indefinitely without corpus refresh and graph regeneration.
The third external-validity threat is a scope restriction on what counts as legal
knowledge. The corpus covers codified law and closely related statutory instruments. It
does not include doctrinal commentary, ministerial circulars, scholarly interpretation, or
case law. Algerian legal practice, like legal practice elsewhere, draws on more than codified text alone. In procedural, constitutional, commercial, and administrative contexts
especially, authoritative interpretation may depend not only on the black-letter provision
but also on commentary, implementing texts, or judicial practice. The present system
does not model that broader legal ecology. It answers about codified law only. This
is consistent with the methodological scope established earlier in the thesis: the project
addresses statutory legal reasoning first, not the totality of Algerian legal practice. The
resulting claims should therefore be read as claims about codified-law assistance, not as
full-spectrum legal decision support.
These external-validity limits define the boundary of generalization. The study supports conclusions about a structured, codified, benchmarked slice of Algerian law under

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

77

a fixed corpus snapshot. It does not yet support fine-grained claims about every small
doctrinal category, about post-freeze legislative change, or about legal reasoning over
commentary and case law.

3.6.3

Construct Validity

A final threat concerns whether the chosen metrics measure the construct the thesis
actually cares about, namely legally correct and justified answering. The strongest example is Citation F1. Citation F1 is central to this thesis because it measures whether
the system retrieves and emits the expected article-level support. However, a citation
can be correct while the legal interpretation built on it is wrong. In that sense, Citation
F1 measures retrieval correctness and support alignment more than full legal correctness.
This limitation is not incidental; it is the metric-level version of the broader argument
already made in §1.2.2 and throughout the benchmark chapter: legal reasoning is not a
single skill and cannot be reduced to one observable quantity (Guha et al., 2023).
The methodology partially addresses this limitation in three complementary ways.
First, the reasoning-chain score (Eq. 3.12) evaluates whether the generated inferential
structure resembles the benchmark’s annotated four-step legal reasoning chain. Second,
the answer-faithfulness gate (Eq. 3.15) checks whether each claim in the answer is entailed by at least one cited article through a multilingual NLI score above the calibrated
threshold. Third, the argument-mining faithfulness metric (Eq. 3.16) checks entailment
against the Toulmin-extracted ground of each cited article rather than against the article
text as a whole. Together these three measures move the evaluation from “did the model
cite the right article?” toward “did the model infer the right thing from the cited article,
and from the right part of it?” Even so, they remain proxies. They are still automated
approximations of legal reasoning quality, not direct expert adjudications of full legal correctness. For that reason, the qualitative analysis in §3.5.5 is not a decorative add-on. It
is the place where construct validity is examined most directly, through expert reading of
trajectories, explanations, abstentions, and failure cases rather than through automated
scores alone.

3.7

Conclusion

This chapter defined the methodological basis of the proposed system. It first presented
the structured data layer: a multimodal Algerian legal corpus composed of source PDF
files where available, oﬀicial text companions, Akoma Ntoso XML files, and RDF/Turtle
graph representations. The live corpus contains 171 files in total: 35 source PDF files, 45
text companions, 45 Akoma Ntoso XML files, and 46 RDF/Turtle files, with legal material

Chapter 3. System Architecture and Proposed Approach

78

spanning from 1963 to 2025. The Akoma Ntoso layer is a central contribution of the thesis:
it converts oﬀicial Algerian legal texts into manually audited, article-structured, identifierstable legal objects. This corpus provides the article registry, canonical identifiers, graph
relations, and provenance constraints on which retrieval, citation verification, temporal
reasoning, and benchmark evaluation depend.
The chapter then introduced AlgerianLegalBench as the evaluation layer of the thesis.
The benchmark contains 244 questions across 23 legal categories and eight query types,
with Arabic as the dominant language and French retained as a smaller cross-lingual
slice. Its gold signals include expected documents, expected articles, answerability labels,
reference answers, reasoning chains, and annotation metadata, making it suitable for
evaluating retrieval, citation faithfulness, answer quality, abstention, and reasoning-chain
alignment under Algerian-law conditions.
Finally, the chapter described the proposed typed-handler architecture and the evaluation protocol used to assess it. The system combines deterministic legal infrastructure
with bounded generative reasoning: retrieval, graph traversal, article-registry checks, submodel workers, argument-aware evidence structuring, and faithfulness gates are exposed
as controlled primitives rather than as an unconstrained generation pipeline. The evaluation methodology separates retrieval, citation, generation, faithfulness, abstention, and
runtime telemetry, and reports them globally and across benchmark strata. The results
of this methodology are reported in Chapter 4.

4.5.3

Classifier-Typed Deployability Cost

The locked configuration routes queries through the Gemma-4-31B classifier rather
than reading the benchmark gold query type. The deployability cost of this choice is
most cleanly isolated at the Phase B configuration, where both classifier-typed and goldtyped runs were executed under identical conditions otherwise. The classifier-typed Phase
B configuration reaches CitF1 = 0.298; the gold-typed Phase B configuration reaches
CitF1 = 0.313; the cost is therefore −0.015 absolute, or roughly five percent relative. The
classifier accuracy on which this cost depends is 69.7%, with the weakest pair being exactarticle versus rule-application (F1 = 0.525 for EA), which is the annotation-dependent
boundary noted in §4.3.1. The multi-hop class accounts for the largest single per-type loss:
ten of twenty-six multi-hop questions are misrouted, predominantly to rule-application,
where the Fix-MH consensus aggregation does not apply, and the per-type multi-hop
Citation F1 drops from 0.175 (gold-typed) to 0.100 (classifier-typed). The remainder of
the deployability cost is distributed near-uniformly across the other types.

invariant is stronger than any single language-model generation pass and is one of the
core safety properties of the architecture.

4.6

Stratified Analysis

The aggregate position of AKN-RLM established in §4.3 hides as much as it reveals.
A legal system can improve overall while still failing on the exact slices that motivated
its design, or while paying for those gains on slices that look auxiliary in aggregate. The
present section decomposes the locked result across the four stratification dimensions
routinely computed on every run — query type, diﬀiculty, language, and the abstention sub-analysis on the unanswerable subset — and then reports the telemetry needed
to interpret the cost–quality trade-off. Per-category breakdown across the twenty-three
benchmark categories is deferred to the released artefact bundle, because all but the five
largest categories carry single-digit counts on which category-specific claims would be
statistically fragile.

reading of the chapter: the system’s value lies not in a single monolithic prompt, but in the

bounded recursion, and faithfulness gates.

4.9

Discussion

The preceding sections reported the locked AKN-RLM result against external baseline
tiers, decomposed it into component-level contributions, stratified it across the analytic dimensions of the benchmark, evaluated new gate and robustness diagnostics, and grounded
the results in qualitative trajectories and an LLM-as-judge expert-proxy validation. The
present section reads those results together. Four cross-cutting themes recur across the
chapter: structural heterogeneity matters more than parametric scale; retrieval is the
main ceiling; mechanisms must be evaluated by where they actually help; and abstention
is a first-class legal behaviour rather than a fallback path.

Chapter 4. Integrating AI for Citation-Faithful Legal Reasoning

4.9.1

103

Structural Heterogeneity, Not Parametric Scale

The clearest cross-tier reading of §4.4 is that backbone size does not predict citation
correctness on this benchmark. The largest direct LLM baseline, Llama 3.3 at 70 billion
parameters, also has the highest hallucinated-citation rate at HCR = 0.803; the smallest,
Llama 3.2 at 3 billion, has the lowest HCR among open models at 0.492. Citation F1 itself
is uncorrelated with parameter count across the seven Tier-1 baselines, ranging from 0.030
for Aya Expanse at 8 billion to 0.186 for Gemini 2.5 Flash whose published size is not
disclosed. The correlation that does survive is between confidence without grounding
and parameter count: the larger the open-weight backbone, the more confidently it cites,
and the more often it cites provisions that do not exist (Blair-Stanek and Van Durme,
2025).
AKN-RLM’s locked configuration sits above every Tier-1 baseline on Citation F1 by
at least 1.64× while running a smaller root controller than several baselines. The architectural explanation is not that AKN-RLM has more parameters, but that it has more legal
structure: typed-handler dispatch, a deterministic citation registry, a knowledge-graph
substrate for amendment and temporal relations, Toulmin ADU extraction, bounded
recursive refinement, and faithfulness gates. The no-jurisdiction and no-faithfulness ablations strengthen this reading. The jurisdiction gate mainly protects the unanswerable
and foreign-law subset, while the faithfulness gate mainly benefits rule-application and
multi-hop reasoning. Reliability is therefore produced by several specialized mechanisms,
not by a single general prompt.

4.9.2

The Retrieval Ceiling

A more uncomfortable reading runs through the same data. Mean reciprocal rank over
articles in the locked configuration is 0.310, and Recall@10 over articles is 0.258. That is,
the gold article appears in the system’s article-level top-ten on only about a quarter of
queries. Citation recall, which is bounded above by retrieval recall, is 0.260 and closely
tracks the Recall@10 figure. The architectural lift over the strongest deterministic baseline
on Citation F1 is 2.9×, but the underlying retrieval lift, measured by article-level MRR,
is only 1.28× (0.242 → 0.310). Most of what the typed-handler architecture contributes
is therefore not retrieving the right article more often, but extracting more value from the
articles it does retrieve through verification, ranking, claim-level grounding, and recursive
refinement (Robertson and Zaragoza, 2009; Karpukhin et al., 2020).
The French stress test and the E3 paraphrase variation sharpen this retrieval-ceiling
argument. French queries cause article Recall@10 to fall by 0.160, while E3 paraphrase
raises Recall@10 by only 0.010 without improving final Citation F1. Together, these

Chapter 4. Integrating AI for Citation-Faithful Legal Reasoning

104

results show that the bottleneck is not simply the number of retrieved candidates. It is
the alignment between query language, legal terminology, article structure, and verifier
ranking. The natural implication is to lift and calibrate the candidate pool itself —
through a corpus-tuned dense embedder, Arabic legal text normalization, exact articlenumber matching, French-to-Arabic query translation, bilingual indexing, and a re-ranker
between channel fusion and verifier selection.

4.9.3

Mechanism Shipped Is Not Mechanism Helping Everywhere

The third theme is the cleanest honest framing the chapter defends. Phase D shipped
the gap-driven recursive refinement that the thesis title claims, and the recursion trace
recorded in predictions.jsonl shows the gap-probe reaching depth two or three on 45.1%
of the 244 queries, including 80.8% of multi-hop queries and 100% of the seven temporalfactual queries. The mechanism is therefore demonstrably exercised on the benchmark.
Its observable contribution to the headline Citation F1 metric, however, is bounded by
the same retrieval ceiling described above.
The new no-faithfulness-gate ablation makes this point sharper. The faithfulness
gate and corrective retry are useful for rule-application and multi-hop questions, where
removing the gate reduces Citation F1 by 0.035 and 0.030, respectively. But they are
not a universal improvement: layman-language questions improve by 0.043 without the
corrective retry. This suggests that future versions should apply corrective retry selectively,
keeping it active for rule-application and multi-hop reasoning while relaxing it for the
Darja-to-MSA layman path.
The same mechanism-specific reading applies to the KG and MultiQuery results. The
generic KG-CONTAINS channels are disabled because they regress the locked configuration on this corpus; MultiQuery paraphrase raises retrieval-only metrics but dilutes final
citation precision. These are not implementation failures. They are architectural-learning
results: a mechanism can be structurally plausible and still fail to help if its score band,
lexical hooks, or reranking position are misaligned with the legal corpus.

4.9.4

Abstention as a First-Class Legal Behaviour

The fourth theme is the most legally specific. Forty of the 244 benchmark questions
are unanswerable infection traps designed to test whether a system imports foreign-law
doctrine when Algerian law contains no equivalent. Six of the seven direct LLM baselines
fall into more than twelve of those traps; Qwen 2 falls into twenty-seven of them (JIR =
0.675). Gemini 2.5 Flash, the strongest direct LLM on Citation F1, still imports foreign

Chapter 4. Integrating AI for Citation-Faithful Legal Reasoning

105

law on fifteen of the forty (JIR = 0.375). AKN-RLM holds JIR = 0.000 and reaches
abstention F1 0.703 when the forty traps are treated as the positive class within the full
benchmark. The metric is structurally unavailable to the baseline tiers because they were
not designed to abstain.
The no-jurisdiction-gate ablation strengthens rather than weakens this claim. When
the gate is disabled, abstention recall falls from 0.761 to 0.687, and unanswerable Citation
F1 falls by 0.075. This confirms that abstention is not merely a side effect of weak retrieval. It is an explicitly engineered legal safety behaviour. The qualitative unanswerable
trajectory shows the same pattern at case level: the jurisdiction gate fires before ordinary
retrieval and prevents an infected foreign-law question from being answered with wrong
Algerian citations.
Two qualifications remain. First, abstention precision is lower than abstention recall
(0.654 versus 0.761), so the system still abstains on some answerable questions. This is the
cost of taking faithfulness verification seriously under imperfect retrieval. Second, the nojurisdiction-gate ablation reveals a measurement caveat: JIR remains numerically zero in
the ablated run because the metric implementation uses the same infection detector that
the ablation disables. A future metric pass should compute jurisdictional infection with
an independent detector so that the measurement remains valid even when the deployed
gate is removed.

4.9.5

Summary of the Four Themes

The four themes are not independent. The retrieval ceiling explains why wider candidate pools do not automatically improve Citation F1; the structural heterogeneity argument explains why a smaller-backbone typed-handler system beats larger generic baselines;
the mechanism-specific analysis explains why corrective retry should be query-type-aware;
and the abstention argument explains why a fluent answer is not enough in legal AI. Together they support the thesis-level claim that article-level legal faithfulness is reached
through architecture rather than scale, and through a heterogeneous metric set rather
than through any single number.

4.10

Empirical Threats to Validity

The first empirical threat is small-stratum reliability under language-model
non-determinism. AKN-RLM is mediated by language-model endpoints whose temperature is set to zero but whose output is not bit-for-bit reproducible across calls. Re-running
the locked configuration with identical inputs and seeds produced Citation F1 swings of

Chapter 4. Integrating AI for Citation-Faithful Legal Reasoning

106

approximately ±0.02 overall and up to ±0.05 on small strata such as the seven temporalfactual and twelve conceptual-definitional questions. The chapter therefore treats smallslice results as diagnostic signals rather than as stable standalone headline claims.
The second empirical threat is endpoint drift and the absence of snapshot
pinning. The provider-hosted language-model endpoints used by the locked configuration — gpt-oss-120b, Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking, and google/gemma-4-31B — are exposed
through the AI Grid gateway, which returns the most recent provider checkpoint rather
than a versioned snapshot. Future re-runs of the same code against the same configuration
may therefore be scored against silently different models. The released artefact bundle
records the access date of each endpoint, but the access date is metadata rather than a
recoverable model state.
The third empirical threat is corpus-specific generalization of the architecturallearning findings. The generic KG-CONTAINS feature flags are empirically disabled
in the locked configuration because they regress Citation F1 on this corpus or fail to
fire where intended. This finding is honest for the Algerian Legal Knowledge Graph
used here, whose chapter URIs are often numeric rather than title-bearing. It should
not be overread as a universal claim that KG-augmented retrieval cannot work in legal
IR. A different graph extraction strategy or a different jurisdictional corpus could yield a
different result.
The fourth empirical threat is cross-lingual fragility. The original language slice
already showed that the twelve native French questions are under-served. The full Frenchtranslated benchmark confirms that the problem is structural: Citation F1 falls by 0.100,
article MRR by 0.176, and article Recall@10 by 0.160 when the query language changes
while the corpus remains Arabic-primary. The system should therefore not be described
as a robust bilingual legal assistant in its current form. It is an Arabic-primary system
with partially multilingual model components.
The fifth empirical threat is jurisdiction-metric dependence. The no-jurisdictiongate ablation shows that removing the gate reduces abstention recall from 0.761 to 0.687
and reduces unanswerable Citation F1 by 0.075, confirming that the jurisdiction gate
is load-bearing. However, the reported jurisdictional-infection rate remains 0.000 in the
ablated run because the metric implementation uses the same infection detector that is
disabled in the ablation. The correct future fix is to compute JIR with an independent
detector during metric evaluation, separate from the deployed gate under test.
The sixth empirical threat is expert-validation scope. The LLM-as-judge validation
is useful because it scores correctness, faithfulness, citation accuracy, completeness, and
readability on a 32-question stratified sample. However, it remains a model-based expert
proxy, not a human legal-expert review. It should therefore be read as an additional
diagnostic layer rather than as final legal validation. A human Algerian-law expert review
remains necessary before any real-world deployment claim.

Conclusion

This chapter reported the empirical behaviour of the architecture introduced in Chapter 3 against the 244-question AlgerianLegalBench benchmark under the metric, stratification, and statistical commitments fixed in §3.5. The locked deployable configuration reaches Citation F1 0.305 on the full benchmark with hallucinated-citation rate and
jurisdictional-infection rate both at zero, abstention F1 of 0.703, and argument-mining
faithfulness of 0.471. It exceeds the strongest direct LLM baseline by 1.64× on Citation
F1, the strongest deterministic retrieval baseline by 2.9×, and the strongest minimal-RAG
configuration by 1.74×, while being the only compared system designed for abstention.
The chapter’s argument did not rest on those numbers alone. It rested on the structural pattern they form when read across phase progression, baseline comparison, component ablation, gate diagnostics, stratified analysis, qualitative trajectories, and the LLMas-judge expert-proxy validation. The new defence-week diagnostics sharpen the contribution claims. The jurisdiction gate is load-bearing for the unanswerable and foreign-law
safety path; the faithfulness gate and corrective retry help rule-application and multi-hop
reasoning but should be relaxed for layman-language questions; MultiQuery paraphrase
slightly improves retrieval-only metrics without improving final citation precision; and
the French-translated benchmark confirms that the current deployment is Arabic-primary
rather than robustly cross-lingual.
The chapter has also been explicit about what these results do not support. Articlelevel retrieval remains below 0.30 Recall@10; conceptual-definitional questions remain
weak; French access is not yet robust; generic KG-CONTAINS retrieval is disabled after
negative additive ablations on this corpus; abstention precision records the genuine cost
of conservative verification; and the expert-proxy validation does not replace human legal
assessment. These limitations do not undermine the thesis contribution. They define the
next engineering and research priorities: a corpus-tuned dense embedder, Arabic legal
normalization and exact article matching, French-to-Arabic or bilingual retrieval, querytype-specific corrective retry, independent jurisdiction-infection measurement, and human
legal-expert validation.
The next chapter reads these results against the broader contribution claims of the
thesis — the benchmark, the architecture, the Akoma Ntoso corpus, and the legal knowledge graph together — and names how those remaining bottlenecks can be pushed in
future work.

5.3.1.1 Problem Statement
LexAlgeria addresses three connected problems in the current Algerian legal-information
ecosystem.
• Fragmented access to legal sources: Algerian legal information is distributed
across oﬀicial journals, codes, ministerial portals, databases, and scanned or weakly

LexAlgeria provides a multilingual legal AI platform with three functional layers:
• Access layer: a web interface and backend API through which users submit questions in Arabic, French, or English.
• Grounding layer: retrieval over a structured Algerian legal corpus, with documentlevel and article-level evidence selection.
• Control layer: recursive response generation, citation verification, hallucination
control, jurisdictional-scope checking, and abstention when the system lacks suﬀicient support.
This architecture converts the technical contributions of the thesis into product value.

5.4.1.2 Cost Structure
The main costs can be divided into initial investment costs and recurring operating costs.
The initial investment covers the transition from prototype to deployable service. Recurring costs cover the continuous operation and improvement of the platform.
The most sensitive cost driver is inference. If latency is high or the recursive generation
layer requires too many model calls per answer, cloud costs can increase faster than
revenue. For this reason, latency in milliseconds is not only a technical metric; it is
also a financial metric. The final deployment should therefore monitor average latency,
model-call count per answer, cache hit rate, and cost per query.

Mitigation Strategy and Financial Implications

Risk mitigation has a direct financial cost. Corpus maintenance, expert validation,

the platform credible for professional and institutional buyers. In this sense, compliance
and reliability are not secondary costs; they are part of the product’s value proposition.
The most important mitigation measures are:
• Technical mitigation: retrieval grounding, citation verification, recursive answer
control, jurisdictional filtering, abstention, and latency optimization.
• Legal mitigation: clear terms of use, classification as legal information rather
than legal advice, user disclaimers, and contractual limitation of liability.
• Operational mitigation: corpus update procedures, expert review workflows,
monitoring dashboards, incident logs, and customer-support escalation.
• Financial mitigation: tiered pricing, setup fees for customized deployments,
cloud-cost monitoring, and prioritization of high-value B2B/B2G clients.
The conclusion of this section is that LexAlgeria is financially plausible only if the
technical system continues to produce measurable reliability gains. The business model
is not sustained by the novelty of AI alone, but by the ability to reduce legal-search

5.5

Conclusion

This chapter examined LexAlgeria as the economic and strategic extension of the technical system developed in this thesis. The analysis showed that the platform is positioned
in an emerging legal-tech market where the main opportunity is not simply the automation of legal text generation, but the provision of trustworthy, citation-grounded, and
multilingual access to Algerian law.
The market analysis identified four principal user segments: public institutions, enterprises, legal professionals, and citizens. Each segment expresses a different version of
the same underlying need: faster access to reliable legal information, lower dependence
on fragmented sources, and better usability for users who do not master legal search techniques. The competitive analysis further showed that LexAlgeria should not be positioned
as a replacement for lawyers or as a generic chatbot, but as a controlled legal-information
layer built around Algerian legal sources, citation verification, jurisdictional safety, and
responsible abstention.
The proposed business model follows a hybrid structure combining freemium citizen
access, paid professional subscriptions, enterprise and institutional plans, and setup or
customization fees. This tiered strategy makes it possible to preserve the social objective
of legal accessibility while relying economically on higher-value B2B and B2G segments.
Operationally, the MVP remains limited to a web interface and backend API, while mobile
applications, advanced enterprise integrations, and extended professional workflows are
treated as later commercial developments.
The financial and risk analysis highlighted that the sustainability of LexAlgeria depends directly on technical reliability. Metrics such as Cite F1, Doc Cite F1, MRR,
R@10, P@10, HCR-down, JIR-down, Abst F1, and latency are therefore not only
experimental measures; they are also business indicators. They determine whether the
platform can build trust, reduce legal-information risk, control operating costs, and justify
professional or institutional adoption. The final numerical values and improvements over
baseline systems should be inserted once the experimental evaluation is finalized.
Overall, the chapter establishes that LexAlgeria has a coherent strategic foundation:
a clearly identified market gap, a differentiated technical value proposition, a realistic
tiered revenue model, and an explicit risk-control strategy. Its commercial feasibility
remains conditional on three requirements: maintaining an up-to-date and structured
Algerian legal corpus, demonstrating measurable reliability gains over baseline systems,
and preserving a clear boundary between legal information and legal advice. If these
conditions are satisfied, the project can evolve from an academic prototype into a credible

General Conclusion
This thesis addressed the problem of reliable legal reasoning in Algerian law through
artificial intelligence. Its starting point was the observation that legal reasoning cannot
be reduced to fluent answer generation. In legal contexts, an answer must be grounded
in identifiable sources, supported by valid citations, bounded by the correct jurisdiction,
sensitive to legal hierarchy and temporality, and able to abstain when the available corpus
does not justify a response. These requirements are particularly important in the Algerian
context, where legal information is multilingual, structurally complex, historically layered,
and comparatively underrepresented in existing legal-AI resources.
The central research question of the thesis was therefore how RAG, KG construction,
recursive orchestration, and AM can be combined to support citation-faithful legal reasoning over Algerian law. The answer developed throughout the thesis is that no single
technique is suﬀicient on its own. Retrieval can locate candidate provisions, but it does
not guarantee that the correct article has been selected. A knowledge graph can expose
legal structure, amendment relations, and cross-references, but it does not decide how
a complex question should be decomposed or when evidence is insuﬀicient. A language
model can synthesize answers, but without strict controls it remains vulnerable to hallucinated citations, unsupported claims, and jurisdictional contamination. The contribution
of this work is therefore not a single isolated model, but an integrated architecture in
which each layer compensates for a specific weakness of the others.
The first contribution of the thesis is the construction of a structured Algerian legal
corpus. The final live dataset contains 171 files, totaling 189.46 MB: 35 source PDF
files, 45 plain-text companions, 45 Akoma Ntoso XML files, and 46 RDF/Turtle files,
including one consolidated legal knowledge graph. The corpus spans legal material from
1963 to 2025 and covers major Algerian codes, constitutional texts, framework statutes,
sectoral legislation, and amending instruments. This data layer transforms Algerian legal documents from publication-oriented files into machine-readable legal objects, with
explicit article boundaries, identifiers, metadata, references, and graph relations. It also
provides the registry needed to verify whether a cited legal provision actually exists.
The second contribution is AlgerianLegalBench, a benchmark designed specifically
for evaluating legal reasoning over Algerian law. The benchmark contains 244 ques127

General Conclusion

128

tions across 23 legal categories and 8 query types: exact-article, rule-application,
multi-hop, temporal-factual, conceptual-definitional, layman-language, long-context, and
unanswerable questions. Its design reflects the thesis’s methodological position that legalAI evaluation must go beyond answer fluency. Each item includes gold legal documents,
expected article references, answerability metadata, reasoning chains, and annotation
information. The benchmark therefore evaluates not only whether a system produces a
plausible answer, but whether it retrieves, cites, and reasons from the correct legal sources.
The third contribution is the AKN-RLM architecture. The system combines typed
query handling, hybrid retrieval, knowledge-graph traversal, bounded recursive orchestration, Toulmin-inspired ADU extraction, and a triple faithfulness gate. The first gate
verifies citation existence against the article registry. The second gate controls jurisdictional contamination through canary detection and boundary checking. The third gate
verifies claim-level support using multilingual NLI. When the system cannot verify an
answer, it performs one corrective regeneration step and, if necessary, abstains. This
design operationalizes a central principle of the thesis: in legal settings, an honest refusal
is preferable to an unverifiable answer.
The empirical results confirm the value of this structural approach. On the full 244question benchmark, the locked deployable AKN-RLM configuration reaches a Citation F1
of 0.305, with hallucinated-citation rate and jurisdictional-infection rate both at 0.000.
It obtains an abstention F1 of 0.703 on the unanswerable infection-trap subset and an
argument-mining faithfulness score of 0.471. Compared with the strongest direct-LLM
baseline, it improves Citation F1 by approximately 1.64×. Compared with the strongest
deterministic retrieval baseline, it improves Citation F1 by approximately 2.9×, and compared with the strongest minimal-RAG configuration, it improves it by approximately
1.74×. These results support the main empirical claim of the thesis: citation-faithful
legal reasoning is improved more by structured orchestration and verification than by
relying on parametric scale alone.
The results also clarify where the system succeeds and where it remains limited. The
strongest improvements come from retrieval, typed-handler dispatch, registry-bounded
citation verification, and the abstention path. Pervasive Toulmin extraction contributes
not only to answer generation, but also to the possibility of measuring argument-mining
faithfulness. Recursive refinement is exercised on a substantial portion of the benchmark,
especially multi-hop and temporal questions, but its marginal effect on the headline Citation F1 remains bounded by the underlying retrieval ceiling. Similarly, several KGCONTAINS channels were implemented and tested, but they did not improve the locked
configuration on the current corpus. This is an important result in itself: the mere presence of a knowledge graph does not guarantee retrieval improvement unless the graph
representation exposes the lexical and structural hooks needed by the retrieval process.
Several limitations must therefore be kept in view. First, article-level retrieval remains

General Conclusion

129

the main bottleneck: article Recall@10 stays below 0.30, which limits the maximum citation recall that the rest of the architecture can achieve. Second, the French-language slice
is clearly under-served, mainly because French queries are matched against an Arabicprimary corpus with limited direct lexical overlap. Third, abstention precision remains
imperfect: the system sometimes abstains on answerable questions when the faithfulness
gate cannot verify the generated answer. Fourth, the jurisdictional canary gate must be
interpreted with caution because part of its signal is derived from the same infectiontrap surface forms used in evaluation. Finally, automated metrics such as Citation F1,
argument-mining faithfulness, and reasoning-chain alignment remain proxies for legal correctness; they cannot fully replace expert legal assessment.
The economic chapter extended the technical work into a strategic business perspective through the proposed LexAlgeria platform. The analysis showed that the system has
a plausible value proposition as a citation-faithful, multilingual legal-information platform for citizens, legal professionals, enterprises, and public institutions. However, its
feasibility is directly conditional on the same technical properties evaluated in the thesis:
reliable retrieval, valid citations, low hallucination, jurisdictional discipline, controlled
abstention, and acceptable latency. LexAlgeria should therefore be understood not as
a generic chatbot or a substitute for lawyers, but as a controlled legal-information layer
whose commercial credibility depends on measurable reliability and a clear boundary between legal information and legal advice.
Future work should proceed in several directions.
The first priority is to improve the retrieval layer through a corpus-tuned dense embedder,
richer hybrid retrieval, and more robust article-level ranking.
The second is to expand French-language coverage, either by aligning French queries
with Arabic legal provisions more effectively or by enriching the corpus with parallel and
French-facing legal material.
The third is to redesign the graph-retrieval interface so that knowledge-graph relations
contribute more directly to candidate selection, especially for multi-hop, temporal, and
amendment-sensitive questions.
The fourth is to run the planned no-jurisdiction-gate and no-NLI-gate ablations in
order to isolate the exact contribution of each faithfulness component.
The fifth is to complement automated evaluation with expert legal review, especially
for diﬀicult multi-hop, long-context, and interpretively ambiguous questions.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates that reliable legal AI for Algerian law requires
more than larger language models. It requires better legal infrastructure: structured
legal data, article-level retrieval, explicit legal relations, controlled recursive reasoning,
argument-aware evidence organization, and strict faithfulness verification. The proposed
framework does not solve all challenges of Algerian legal reasoning, but it establishes a

General Conclusion

130

concrete foundation for studying them: a structured corpus, a legal knowledge graph,
a benchmark, an evaluated architecture, and a realistic path toward deployment. Its
main contribution is therefore both technical and methodological. The reported system
scores are important evidence for the proposed architecture, but they are contingent on
the current corpus, models, and retrieval configuration. The broader lesson is that, in
a low-resource and legally sensitive setting, trustworthy legal AI must be built as an
evidence-controlled system rather than as an unconstrained generator.
